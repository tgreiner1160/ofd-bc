GENERAL OPERATIONS Purpose The Omaha Fire Department has adopted the Midlands Protocol Committee Treatment Protocol and the State of Nebraska EMS Treatment Protocols as a basis for patient care guidelines. This SOP consists of these two protocols with modifications from the Omaha Fire Department Medical Director for Omaha Fire specific policies and procedures. Policy 1. The Omaha Fire Department Medical Director, with the approval of the Fire Chief, is responsible for developing and approving these protocols. The EMS SOP, in its entirety, is considered a standing order. 2. This document incorporates national AHA/PHTLS/ATLS/PALS and other national organization guidelines that refer to specific types of patient care. 3. The Omaha Fire Department will follow all EMS rules and regulations set forth by the Authority Having Jurisdiction. Definitions and Abbreviations Bypassed Patient – A patient that the receiving hospital has chosen to divert to another facility or diverts a trauma patient to the trauma center. emsCHARTS – OFD mechanism for documentation of EMS patients and EMS incidents PCR – patient care report Fire Reports – OFD mechanism for documentation of all fire apparatus activity, medical and fire Receiving Hospital – The hospital that receives the patient Transport Codes and Guidelines Code 1             Minimal or no apparent disease or injury. Patient transported for examination. Code 2             Obvious illness or injury, not a serious injury or illness but needs medical attention.
Code 2 Trauma      Trauma patient with an obvious injury but doesn’t clearly meet the criteria for a Code 3. Enough questionable signs, symptoms or MOI exists to warrant the expertise of the Trauma Center. Code 3             Apparent serious / life-threatening medical illness needing immediate medical attention. Code 3 Trauma      Trauma patient with an actual or potential life or limb threatening injury. Code 3 STEMI       Patient has been identified as having ST-segment elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI alert). All STEMI patients will be transported Code 3 STEMI to a hospital with cardiac catheterization lab capabilities. Code 3 Stroke      Patient has been identified as having a stroke (stroke alert). All stroke patients will be transported Code 3 Stroke to a hospital with stroke care capabilities. Code 99            Cardiopulmonary arrest with CPR and full resuscitation in progress. Code 4             Dead patient. Code 5             For suspected SIDS patient, (patient meets criteria for Code 4, and transport is for the family/bystanders). CPR only (basic life support). Patients will generally be transported to the closest capable and appropriate hospital emergency department. Patients receiving specialized care related to their condition within a certain hospital system, when logistically feasible, should be transported to that facility, or a facility in that hospital system. Examples include LVAD, Transplant, Stroke, STEMI, Behavioral Health, etc. Sound judgement will be utilized when determining the most appropriate facility for transport based on these guidelines. Law Enforcement - Police officers are not permitted the right to dictate the hospital that patients will be transported to by OFD medic units. Trauma Center Rotation for MCI of the Day Schedule: For patients who are hemodynamically unstable or who do not express a hospital preference, transport to:    Nebraska Medicine – Odd Days    CHI Alegent Creighton Health Bergan Mercy – Even Days
MCI (Mass Casualty Incident) – In the event of an MCI, the Omaha Trauma Centers will both open and receive Code 3 (RED) patients with a 4 and 4 rotation. Refer to EMS 5 -0 Multi- Casualty Response SOP. Trauma Center Rotation Protocol. Pediatric Transport Considerations A pediatric patient is a patient up to and including 15 years of age. Pediatric Medical Patients – Code 3 medical pediatric patients should be transported to the closest hospital. Pediatric patients with extreme hypothermia exhibiting signs of altered mental statu s or cardiac dysfunction and no signs or suspicion of trauma should be transported to Children’s Hospital. If the receiving hospital diverts an OFD medic unit attempting to transport a Code 3 medical (non-trauma) pediatric patient, the OFD medic unit should transport the patient based on the medic unit’s actual physical location at the point of being diverted (go to closest of):    If west of 60th Street in Omaha, transport to Children’s Hospital.    If east of 60th Street in Omaha, transport to Nebraska Medicine. Pediatric Trauma Patients – All Code 2 (Level 2) pediatric trauma patients should be transported to either Children’s Hospital or Nebraska Medicine (see below.) All pediatric patients with asphyxiation injuries will be transported to Children’s Medical Center. This protocol applies 24/7 regardless of “trauma center of the day” for adult patients.
The following injured children are best served by evaluation and treatment at Nebraska Medicine. These children must be transported to Nebraska Medicine:    Traumatically injured children who DO require major, immediate resuscitation    Code 99 (traumatic cardiac arrest except asphyxiation – see below)    Hemodynamic instability or poor perfusion    Respiratory compromise, including need to maintain airway and intubation    Neurologic compromise, GCS <9 or deteriorating status    All burn patients    Traumatic amputations    Significant vascular injuries    High risk mechanisms: i.e. ejection, GSW    Unstable pelvic fractures    Spinal cord injury or paralysis “Do Not Resuscitate” (DNR) Orders and Identification of CPR Only (Adopted from “Nebraska EMS Model Protocols”) With the advent of individuals taking greater responsibility for their own health care, decisions made by the individuals themselves, to not prolong their own life, is recognized as valid and has become more common. Health Care Power of Attorney, Living Wills and “Do Not Resuscitate” (DNR) orders are encountered frequently by OFD personnel. Occasionally, a family member will call 911 even when a DNR order exists or a Living W ill is present. There are also times when OFD will arrive on scene, begin resuscitation procedures and a DNR or Living Will is discovered. This protocol is designed to provide guidance for such situations. “Do Not Resuscitate” (DNR) – A DNR is a written order by a physician stating that no cardiopulmonary resuscitation will be initiated. A DNR must be signed by a physician, dated and have the patient’s name on it. OFD EMS personnel can honor a DNR. The OFD EMS provider must be identified in the patient care report. Health Care Power of Attorney (HCPA) – is a legal document stating the name of the person the individual (patient) has named as the person who will make medical decisions for their care. It should be signed by the patient and the patient’s attorne y, and only applies to adults. Living Will – This document states the patient’s wishes should they require resuscitation or life support measures. The document must be signed by the patient and the patient’s physician and only applies to adults.
1. OFD EMS providers will not initiate or continue cardiopulmonary resuscitation on a patient in cardiac arrest once a valid DNR order is confirmed. In the event of uncertainty, resuscitative measures should be initiated. 2. DNR does not mean that emergency medical care for any other medical condition will be changed or limited. Patients shall receive emergency medical treatment (BVM / airway management, IV therapy and pharmacology) up until the point of cardiac arrest. 3. Physicians may designate a patient as DNR by written order, verbally – when the physician is physically present at the scene or by telephone consult from the paramedic on scene to the patient’s physician. 4. A written DNR order must contain the patient’s name and be signed by the physician or by the RN who received the order from the physician. 5. If a physician physically, present at the scene designates the patient as a DNR, the paramedic shall ask the physician to document the DNR designation on the OFD 179 and sign it. If the physician refuses to document and sign the OFD 179, PCR (tablet) the paramedic shall initiate resuscitative measures. 6. In a skilled care facility (nursing home), DNR orders documented in the patient’s medical record are considered valid if signed by the physician or by the RN for th e physician. A DNR form may be used, but is not required in the nursing home setting. The OFD EMS provider who observed the DNR order must be identified on the patient care report. 7. An OFD EMS provider can honor an effective Living Will or Health Care Power of Attorney. This can be directly observed or communicated by a family member. OFD personnel shall have the family member attesting to the presence of the Living Will or HCPA state the presence in writing and sign the document to be filed with the patient care report. OFD EMS providers can presume the validity of either of these documents if signed in Nebraska. Documents from other states in compliance with that state’s laws are also valid in Nebraska. 8. If the family desires CPR and/or resuscitation in the presence of a DNR or HCPA, the family’s wishes shall be honored. 9. Observation of an original or a photocopy of a living will or health care power of attorney must be documented in the patient care report. An OFD EMS provider shall not honor a living will if there is no information or evidence that a physician has determined the patient is in a terminal condition or in a persistent vegetative state. If there is information or evidence that a physician has determined the patient is in a terminal condition or in a persistent vegetative state, this information should be documented in the patient care report. The patient care report must also contain information that the patient is an adult (is 19 or older or has been married).
10. If a telephone consult with the patient’s physician or the physician’s designee verifies a DNR, the OFD paramedic can honor the order. Authorization shall be documented on the patient care report and include the physician’s or physician designee’s name, telephone number and time of the telephone call from the paramedic to the physician. 11. Once CPR has been initiated, resuscitative measures may be discontinued when any one of the following occurs: a. A DNR or no code order is confirmed. b. A Living Will or HCPA for an adult is being followed. c. A physician physically present at the scene or the medical director, based on information from the OFD EMS provider on scene, determines that CPR is futile or should be discontinued. d. An OFD EMS provider is following termination of CPR protocols that have been authorized by the OFD Physician Medical Director. e. Any time the scene becomes unsafe for rescuers. Resuscitation of a DNR Patient – If inadvertently, a DNR patient is resuscitated, and in the absence of physician directives, care should be continued and the patient should be taken to the catchment area hospital. Notification to OFD Dispatch – Once resuscitative measures are terminated, notify OFD dispatch and request an OPD cruiser if one has not already been dispatched. OFD personnel will remain on scene until OPD arrives. Give a brief history to the officer. Obtain the officer’s name and badge number and document in the electronic patient care report in the narrative section. Code 4 Patients OFD personnel may be called to a scene where biologic and clinical death is evident. In such cases, it is not necessary to begin resuscitation. Situations may also occur where CPR has been initiated on an obviously deceased patient prior to the arrival of OFD EMS personnel. This protocol is intended to assist OFD personnel in the identification of such situations.    Biologic Death – cell death as a result of lack of circulating oxygen and nutrients. Biologic death begins at 6 minutes for the brain at normal temperatures.    Clinical Death – pulseless, apneic and unresponsive patient.    Postmortem lividity – is the discoloration of the body from blood seeping from the tissues and settling in the dependent parts of the body.    Putrefaction – decomposition of body tissues. Depending on temperature conditions, this occurs sometime between 40 and 96 hours after death.
   Rigor Mortis – a biologic process where body muscles stiffen as a result of normal chemical changes that occur with cell death. The process develops first in the face and jaw, gradually extending downward until the body is in full rigor. Rigor mortis occurs sometime between 2 to 12 hours after death. If the patient meets code 4 criteria, OFD EMS providers may discontinue CPR or may choose not to initiate CPR. No patient will be declared Code 4 without a complete, hands-on physical evaluation including: •   No pulse, respirations or blood pressure •   No response to painful stimulation, no corneal reflex, and no response to a sternal rub •   A one minute cardiac rhythm strip will be obtained which confirms asystole (ALS unit must provide) •   *All of the above will be documented in the patient care report (PCR) The only exceptions to the above are the following:    Injury not compatible with life    Advanced whole body decomposition. Remember that smell is NOT an advanced sign of death. Anytime the exceptions are used to declare death, the state of the body should b e documented in the Narrative section of the PCR. NOTE: Care should be taken to rule out hypothermia, acute alcoholic intoxication, and drug overdose. Refusal of Care The patient has the right to refuse either or both care and transportation. However, OFD personnel have both a moral and legal obligation to provide adequate medical care, according to the provider’s level of training and certification, until the patient is delivered to the receiving hospital. In situations in which the patient is refusing care, the following guidelines shall be followed: 1. Adults a. An adult is an individual 19 years old or older or who is or has been married (NEB REV STAT §43-2101). A competent adult can refuse medical services and/or transportation to a health care facility. b. A legal guardian can consent to or refuse medical services and/or transportation to a health care facility for an incompetent adult.
c. A person appointed as a Health Care Power of Attorney can consent or refuse consent for medical services and/or transportation to a health care facility for the incompetent adult named in the power of attorney. 2. Minors a. A minor is an individual under 19 years of age that has never been married. b. A parent or legal guardian can consent or refuse consent on behalf of a minor, for medical services and/or transportation to a health care facility. 3. Documentation (required) a. Each patient shall be given a minimal physical assessment consisting of pupil evaluation, level of consciousness (GCS), vital signs, lung sounds and/or respiratory rate and effort and a general head to toe physical exam (palpation as indicated). b. If a patient refuses to submit to a physical exam, the OFD EMS provider in charge of patient care shall document this refusal in the narrative of the electronic patient care report. The narrative shall also document the following: 1. Results of the minimal physical assessment. Fill in the appropriate areas in the patient care report. 2. Visual assessment, for example: “The patient is up and walking at the scene with no apparent injury.” 3. Patient is alert, coherent and articulate, for example: a. Alert – “The patient states his name, location and time of day correctly.” b. Coherent – “The patient is speaking in complete sentences with logical thought flow.” c. Articulate – “Speech is distinct.” Absence of any one of these may indicate insufficient ability to make good decisions. Therefore, all efforts to convince the patient to allow treatment / transport should be attempted. NOTE: ETOH does NOT justify inaction. If after appropriate assessment, treatment is not necessary, contact OPD for transport to appropriate facility. 4. Reason for the patient’s refusal, for example: a. “Patient states he has no pain, injury or medical problem.” or “Patient refuses treatment / transport for religious reasons.” 5. Attempt to get others involved, for example: “Family members also failed in efforts to encourage patient to be transported to the hospital.”
6. Consequences explained, for example: “Patient informed that he may suffer serious physical harm or death as a result of not being treated / transported.” 7. Alternatives explained, for example: “Patient instructed to use alternate means for transport, or if condition worsens or change of mind occurs, to call 911.” 8. Concluding statement to each incident of patient refusal shall be the following: “Patient strongly advised to seek medical care as soon as possible.” 9. Signature of patient and witness on OFD 179 form. A valid witness shall be any family member of legal age, a police officer, bystander, or at the very least, another OFD firefighter on the call. If the patient refuses to sign the refusal form, then the OFD firefighter documenting the run shall note this in the narrative. Complete documentation shall include all applicable portions of the electronic patient care report program and the OFD 179 form. From a legal standpoint, this documentation shall provide the basic defense that appropriate actions were taken. Any omitted patient care documentation can be challenged whether or not appropriate care was actually provided at the time of the incident. NOTE: If a patient needs treatment and/or transport to the hospital but the hospital of choice is NOT a hospital that the OFD medic unit services (i.e. Mercy Hospital, Council Bluffs or Offutt) and the patient refuses transport by OFD, the person in charge shall instruct the patient to call a private ambulance for transport. If a private ambulance service is called, Omaha Fire CAN remain on the scene until the private ambulance arrives to transfer care of the patient. After a patient assessment, and if the patient condition warrants, Omaha Fire can remain on scene to monitor the patient and treat as necessary until the private ambulance has arrived. GENERAL PRINCIPLES Communication Any healthcare provider may contact the catchment/receiving facility or the Omaha Fire Department Medical Director or shift supervisor for further direction or confirmation of orders whenever the patient’s condition or the situation warrants. Body Substance Isolation (BSI) It is protocol policy to practice body substance isolation when caring for ALL patients. This means wearing gloves and protective eyewear when administering patient care. BSI shall be observed when handling blood and body fluids on surfaces or items soiled by blood and body fluids; masks and protective eyewear during procedures likely to generate droplets of blood and body fluids; and aprons or gowns during procedures likely to generate splashes of blood
or body fluid. This includes washing hands after each patient care incident even if gloves were worn or waterless soap was used. All personnel should don N-95 masks when in contact with patients in where an airborne or potential blood-borne communicable disease is suspected or confirmed by history, this includes the potential for blood splatter or active coughing. Examples of some communicable diseases include (e.g. Tuberculosis, Hepatitis, Influenza, Meningitis etc). Restraints Purpose – OFD personnel occasionally encounter patients who require transport to a receiving hospital but who also pose a threat to themselves, others or to OFD personnel. Because of this threat of physical harm, it may be impossible to transport without restraining the patient. This protocol is intended to provide guidance in the use of restraints and a procedure to follow in such cases. Definitions/Abbreviations Four-point restraints – a restraint applied to each extremity. Two-point restraints – restraints applied to two extremities, usually both arms and both legs. Hard restraints – restraints made of leather (leather restraints). Soft restraints – restraints made of material such as a sheet, cloth or tape. Note: OFD personnel/rigs are prohibited from carrying or using metal handcuffs. Indications    A patient who needs to be transported for medical care, who is refusing transport of care, and who is incompetent to refuse.    A person, who appears to be mentally ill and who as a result of such mental illness, appears to be an imminent danger to others (including OFD personnel) or to himself/herself or to be gravely disabled. Precautions / Considerations    Any attempt at restraint involves risk to the patient and to OFD personnel.    The firefighter’s safety must come before patient considerations. Do not attempt to restrain the patient without adequate assistance.    Physical restraints are a last resort. All possible means of verbal persuasion should be attempted first.
   A patient who is alert, oriented, aware of his/her condition, and capable of understanding the consequences of his/her refusal is entitled to refuse treatment. He/she may not be restrained and treated against his/her will. (Review consent guidelines and confer with PSS or medical director if in doubt.)    Any restrained patient may vomit, be prepared to suction and reposition as needed. Once restrained, the patient is never to be left alone. Aspiration can occur if patient is restrained on his/her back and cannot protect his/her own airway.    The airway must be free and accessible for airway control. Do NOT place your fingers, hand or arm directly in front of the patient’s mouth.    Once restraints are considered necessary, decide on either two-point or four-point restraints, based on the situation.    Do not restrain a patient in the prone position.    Do not restrain a patient sandwiched between backboards, scoop stretchers or other immobilization devices.    Do not “hog tie” patients (hands restrained behind back, feet restrained together and the two restrained attached together).    Check restraints as soon as applied and every 10 minutes thereafter to ensure no injury to extremities.    Remove restraints only with sufficient personnel available to control the patient - generally, only in the hospital setting.    Other than primary psychiatric disorders, medical causes of combativeness include hypoglycemia, hypoxia, head injury and drug ingestion.    Written and verbal reports must completely document the necessity for the use of physical restraints. Record condition of extremities before applying restraints and recheck and record condition on arrival at hospital.    Metal handcuffs are not an authorized method of restraint for OFD personnel. The only exception to this policy is for OFD Fire Investigators whom have been specifically trained in the use of handcuffs.    If a patient is already handcuffed by law enforcement, an officer must ride with the patient to the hospital.    Law enforcement should be asked to remove handcuffs as soon as possible for the patient’s own safety. However, if an officer deems handcuffs are necessary, place patient supine on stretcher and secure handcuffs to the base of the stretcher.    Consider utilizing more personnel to restrain patient.
Airway, Oxygen and Ventilation — Adult Patient An intact airway and maintenance of adequate oxygenation and ventilation is essential for all patients with medical or traumatic conditions. Throughout this treatment protocol it is assumed that the OFD personnel will maintain a patent airway and provide appropriate supplemental oxygenation. 1. If ventilating adequately: Nasal cannula, 2-6 lpm or mask non-rebreather at 10 -15 lpm. If unresponsive, maintain patent upper airway with jaw thrust. Consider oral/nasal airway. 2. If not ventilating adequately: BVM with 100% oxygen. If unresponsive, consider oral/nasal airway adjunct. Consider assisting ventilations in those patients whose respiratory status does not improve after receiving oxygen by mask non-rebreather. 3. If vital signs have not improved after initial oxygen and medications re -evaluate oxygen delivery device and adjust accordingly. 4. If pulse oximetry is used, adjust oxygen delivery devices to an oxygen saturation of 95% or above (goal is 100%). Record all vital signs (BP, Pulse, Respirations and Lung Sounds) Adequate Ventilations — Spontaneous ventilations at a rate between 10-30 with all of the following characteristics: normal depth, absence of accessory muscle use, clear lung sounds and no or very little signs of distress. Inadequate Ventilations — Defined as any one of the following: 1. Ventilations at a rate less than 10 or greater than 30; or 2. A ventilation rate between 10 and 30 in the presence of: a. Shallow/labored respirations b. Wheezes, c. Wet sounds or d. Blue, gray or mottled skin Stable — A patient who is awake, alert, BP greater than 90 systolic, no chest pain, warm dry skin. Unstable — A patient who demonstrates any of the following: confused, sluggish, altered mental status, unconscious, BP below 90 systolic, chest pain, cool and clammy skin, dyspnea, or has pulmonary edema.
Standard Operating Procedures                                                                EMS 2-0 IV Therapy If the EMT-B is approved by EMS Training and the OFD Medical Director to start IV’s, the following protocol shall be followed. 1.       If ALS level intervention requires IV access, the IV should be started as soon as possible and, in the case of the trauma patient, IVs should be started enroute to the hospital. 2. CARDIOVASCULAR EMERGENCIES Definitions Stable — Awake, alert, BP greater than 90 systolic, no chest pain, warm dry skin Unstable — confused, sluggish, altered mental status, unconscious, BP below 90 systolic, chest pain, cool and clammy skin, dyspnea, pulmonary edema. Out-Of-Hospital Cardiac Arrest (OHCA) 1. High Quality CPR (HQ-CPR) – If patient is in cardiac arrest on arrival, start or continue basic life support (BLS) for 2 minutes. Priorities are C-A-B, with highest priority given to high-quality chest compressions. Consider Lucas Device, if available. See Procedure J. •   Begin compression at a rate of 100-to-120 per minute with adequate depth of 2- to-2.5 inches. •   Ratio is 30:2 compressions to ventilations until an advanced airway is secured. •   Once an advanced airway is placed, ventilations are 10 per minute (or one ventilation every 6 seconds) using a bag-valve mask with 100% oxygen. Avoid hyperventilating. Apply ResQPOD to tube. See Procedure I. •   ROTATE person doing compressions (compressor) every 2 minutes. •   Ensure no interruptions to compressions of more than 10 seconds for any reason.
•    Do not allow interruptions to compressions to apply pads. Stop compressions to analyze, but resume while AED is charging, and, immediately after shock is delivered. 2. Once AED pads are placed on patient’s bare chest allow AED to analyze and deliver a shock as soon as possible. This is especially important if the arrest is witnessed. (“Prime the pump” with high-quality chest compressions and deliver a shock as quickly as possible, immediately resuming compressions for another 5 cycles or 2 minutes. The best time to rotate compressors is while the AED is analyzing.) 3. Reassess rhythm & pulse check every 5 cycles or 2 minutes of CPR, just prior to delivering the next shock. 4. Limit interruptions of CPR during pulse & rhythm checks to < 10 seconds for airway management and/or medication administration. 5. Once AED is applied, allow machine to deliver shocks as indicated, insuring that all personnel are clear before depressing the shock button. 6. Notify the incoming Medic unit that a code 99 is in progress and standing orders are being followed. Give approximate age of patient and request LUCAS. 7. Consider hypoglycemia and/or OPIOD drug overdose as precipitating factors in cardiopulmonary arrest. 8. Be sure to note and inform paramedics of the real time when all standing orders are performed. Acute Coronary Syndrome (Chest Pain, MI, Suspected Cardiac Event) 1. Airway, Oxygen, vital signs. (The ALS assessment for non-cardiac arrest remains A-B-C, or airways, breathing, circulation.) 2. Position of comfort 3. Monitor vital signs every 5 minutes 4. Consider administering ASA (2 to 4 baby aspirin) 160 – 324 mg PO (chewable), if trained. 5. If the patient is currently prescribed nitroglycerin (NTG) and has not taken any prior to arrival the EMT (if trained) may assist the patient with administration of their NTG. a. Obtain a BP before administering (must be above 100 mmHg systolic) b. Standard dose is one tablet 0.4 mg sublingual (SL) (under the tongue) c. May repeat NTG up to 3 times. Obtain BP prior to each dose. Cease if patient becomes symptomatic of the common side effects of NTG (i.e., headache or hypotension develops)
*Do NOT administer Nitroglycerin if: The patient has used any erectile dysfunction (ED) medication (Viagra/sildenafil, Levitra/vardenafil, and Cialis/tadalafil) within the previous 48 hours. 6. Reassess vital signs including lung sounds after any medication assistance. Dyspnea in the presence of diminished lung sounds, wheezes, rales, or frothy sputum 1. Airway, high flow oxygen (non-rebreather or consider BVM) 2. Position of comfort (45-90 degrees unless severely hypotensive) 3. Administer Nebulized DuoNeb 2.5 mg in 3 mL of saline NOTE: Be cautious of lying patient flat as respiratory distress may increase. ACUTE TRAUMATIC EMERGENCIES General Trauma Management - Priorities for Treatment 1. Airway Management 2. Control the cervical spine. Assume cervical spine injury is present in any patient with: a. Evidence of high impact with a distracting injury. b. Any head or neck injury. c. Neck pain following trauma. d. Altered mental status. e. Presence of any neurological deficit. REMINDER: Loss of sensation or motor activity MAY NOT be present initially with cervical spine fractures. 3. Consider helicopter transport if: a. Incident located North of I-680 or West of 150th Street. b. Transportation by ground to the Trauma Center will be greater than 20 mins. OR c. Extrication time and ground transport time (together) to the Trauma Center will be greater than 20 minutes. d. Consider requesting standby status while you’re in route based on the incident location and information given by dispatch regarding the nature of the call. 4. Helmet Removal:
a. Remove all helmets to avoid airway management problems according to ACS and PHTLS guidelines. The exception may be football helmets with shoulder pads in place. In this case, removal of the helmet is optional, but removal of the face guard is required. Cervical immobilization is without the c-collar, utilizing other resources, i.e. towel rolls, commercial head immobilization devices to stabilize the head. 5. Control external hemorrhage: a. Direct pressure b. Apply a wide band tourniquet 2” above injury for life-threatening extremity hemorrhage, mangled extremity, or traumatic amputation that is not controlled by direct pressure. 6. Hypovolemic shock (assume shock present when pulse greater than 120 and/or systolic BP less than 100 mmHg in a previously normotensive patient; or systolic drops 40-50 mmHg in a previously hypertensive patient, especially if accompanied by pale, clammy skin and decreased level of consciousness). a. Apply oxygen and ventilate if necessary. b. Shock position (feet elevated, head/torso level). Note: Trendelenburg position (supine on a long spine board with the feet-part of the board elevated) is no longer recommended. 7. Fractures/Dislocations - General Principles: a. Check and record peripheral pulses and neurological status before and after manipulating or splinting fractures. b. Apply gentle in-line traction to fractures with the exception of dislocations or fractures involving joints (especially the elbow). c. May straighten and splint severely angulated fractures of extremities with the exception of those involving knee or elbow. For knee or elbow with neurovascular deficit — one attempt to re-align extremity to restore neurovascular status may be attempted. d. May assist with use of traction splint with open or closed femur fractures. e. Immobilize fractures, including joint above and below. f. For suspected fractures of pelvis, consider a sheet to splint the pelvis. 8. DO NOT remove any impaled objects unless obstructing airway or that interfere with CPR. 9. All life or limb-threatening injuries, immediate transport to the Trauma Center. 10. If QuikClot, Celox, or other similar coagulation agent has been applied for bleeding control prior to OFD arrival, leave in place. Do not remove. Follow standard bleeding control measures.

It is important that all OFD personnel involved in a Multi-Casualty Incident be knowledgeable and able to follow these procedures to ensure the most desired outcome possible. This procedure shall be integrated into the overall Incident Management System and may include major transportation incidents, explosions or fire with multiple injuries, hazardous materials incidents with exposure victims, structural collapse incidents, etc. It is the responsibility of the first-arriving company officer to implement these procedures on all incidents requiring the commitment of multiple medic units and other resources. POLICY This policy is to integrate these Multi-Casualty Response procedures within the structure of the Incident Command System. It is the responsibility of the first-arriving company officer to implement these procedures on EMS incidents requiring the commitment of multiple medic units and other resources. General Objectives 1. Establish strong leadership/Command. a. This is the responsibility of the first arriving company. b. Provide for the safety, accountability and welfare of rescue personnel, victims, and bystanders. 3. Assess scene and call for needed resources.

LEVEL I COMMAND STRUCTURE                       (10 - 25 PATIENTS) NOTES 1. Command shall be established by the first arriving company officer, regardless of assignment. Necessary communication of information to 911 Dispatcher is paramount. 2. Triage should be initiated as soon as possible and should take no longer than 30 seconds per patient. 3. It is the responsibility of the Incident Commander to delegate duties as personnel arrive on scene and use discretion to utilize the best possible person for the position (i.e. designate experienced paramedics as Treatment and Transportation Unit Leaders). 4. It is the responsibility of all responding units to remove their EMS equipment from the apparatus and move it to the Treatment Area. 5. All communications to receiving hospitals regarding patient information will be done by the Medical Communications Coordinator(s). This should be the PSS or first arriving Medic Unit Officer. For smaller incidents, the Medical Communications Coordinator may also double as the Transportation Unit Leader. Proper documentation must be followed on forms OFD-56 and 57. 6. The Medical Communications Coordinator(s) will determine the receiving hospital for each patient. Do NOT load patients into waiting ambulances without first making contact with a Medical Communications Coordinator. 7. The Medical Communications Coordinator(s) will make sure that 911 has notified all of the area hospitals of the mass causality incident and the estimated number of patients.
8. Level II Staging shall be implemented and personnel should wait for assignment from the Incident Commander. 9. The Incident Commander will handle all Command and General Staff responsibilities. 10. One Mass Casualty Trailer will be dispatched and report to staging. Mass Casualty Trailers will be identified as MC 201 (OFD Central Station), MC 243 (OFD Station 43), MC 203 (Airport), MC 51 (Ralston), MC 54 (Irvington) and MC 58 (Waterloo). The drivers of the Mass Casualty Trailers will always remain with the apparatus and assist in distribution of supplies to personnel. The Omaha Fire Department’s Mass Casualty Trailers will be transported to the scene by the Firefighter assigned to drive Engine 201 or Engine 243, depending on which Mass Casualty Trailer is dispatched.
LEVEL II COMMAND STRUCTURE                       (26 - 50 PATIENTS) NOTES 1. Level II Staging will be assigned and the first apparatus officer to arrive in the Staging Area will assume the duties of Staging Officer and don the appropriate “Staging” vest located in the B/C vehicle. 2. Treatment Unit will utilize identifiers for the different Treatment Areas (Red, Yellow, and Green). Identifiers will be colored salvage covers, flags, and lights (located on the
Mass Casualty Trailers). The Treatment Unit Leader shall assign Officers for each of the colored Treatment Areas. 3. An Air Operations Coordinator will be assigned if helicopter transport is desired and will respond to the Transportation Officer as “Air Operations”. Air Operations shall include a “Helispot Officer” and appropriate apparatus and manpower necessary to coordinate the landing of helicopter(s). (Formerly known as “LZ” Coordinator) 4. Ambulances should be sent to the Transportation Area/Loading Area from the Staging area no more than two at a time. 5. The Unit Leaders will remain the same as a Level I Multi-Casualty and shall contact their Medical Group Supervisor for additional resources, as needed. 6. Extrication shall include the physical disentanglement and the removal of patients from the scene to the Treatment area. 7. A second Mass Casualty Trailer will be dispatched and report to the Staging Area and await assignment. The drivers of the Mass Casualty Trailers will always remain with the apparatus and assist in distribution of supplies to personnel. 8. The Morgue will fall under the direction of the Triage Unit and will have its own Morgue Manager. Personnel assigned to this area will document injuries and means to identify each patient. Law enforcement shall be notified to secure this area from the public and for investigation purposes. 9. If more than one Medical Communications Coordinator is used, the communications duties to hospitals may be shared. For example: one Coordinator will be responsible for patients transported to Creighton, Immanuel, and Methodist Hospitals, while the second will be responsible for patients transported to the Nebraska Medical Center, Bergan, Lakeside and Midlands Hospitals. 10. Rehab shall include the Salvation Army, Red Cross and at least one ambulance crew. 11. The Treatment Dispatch Manager is to act as a liaison between the Treatment Area Managers (Red, Yellow, and Green Areas) and the Transportation Unit Leader. 12. The Ground Ambulance Coordinator is under the direction of the Transportation Unit but will remain in Staging to assist the Staging Officer with the organization of ambulances needed to be sent to the Transport Area. The Ground Ambulance Coordinator is the liaison between Staging and the Transportation Unit.
LEVEL III COMMAND STRUCTURE                       (MORE THAN 50 PATIENTS) NOTES 1. Due to the size of the incident, it is suggested that the Morgue site is a specific location (such as an airport hangar, warehouse, etc.) and personnel shall still follow through with documentation of identification of each patient. Law enforcement shall be utilized to keep the Morgue Area secure. 2. Multiple Medical Communications Coordinators shall be used and communications to specific hospitals will be assigned.
3. The third Mass Casualty Trailer, upon arrival, will report to the Staging Area. The drivers of the Mass Casualty Trailers will always remain with the apparatus and assist in distribution of supplies to personnel. 4. In the event of a widespread incident, there may be a need to create multiple Medical Groups who would each cover a specific geographic location. Each Medical Group must have fully functional Triage, Treatment and Transport Units, independent of any other Medical Group that may already be established on scene. All Medical Groups will fall under the direction of the Medical Branch Director. Duties will be assigned by the Operation Officer. 5. A centralized Transportation Group is created to organize the many medic units the individual Transport Units will need. Each Medical Group shall have one Transport Unit. 6. An Extrication Branch is created due to the large scale and complexity of the incident. POSITION RESPONSIBILITIES Incident Commander The Incident Commander is the first-arriving company officer. PHYSICIAN ON SCENE There are times that a physician, not affiliated with OFD, but licensed to practice traditional medicine in the state of Nebraska will identify himself/herself on a scene and may wish to direct the actions of OFD EMS personnel. This protocol is intended to provide guidance to OFD EMS personnel while ensuring the best care possible for the patient. When a physician is present on the scene and desires to direct the run, OFD EMS personnel should: 1. Inform the physician that if the physician directs the run, the physician must ride along, in the rear of the Medic Unit with the patient to the hospital and transfer care and treatment history to the emergency room staff. 2. Inform the physician at the outset of the call that OFD paramedic personnel have strict legal guidelines and established protocols and they may not exceed those guidelines or protocols. 3. Inform the physician that any procedure outside of these legal guidelines must be carried out by the physician himself/herself.
4. OFD EMS personnel have the right and obligation at any time there is gross deviation from the accepted protocol to contact the receiving hospital for further instruction. The physician on scene should be informed if contact with the receiving hospital is being made. 5. Only traditional medical practices will be allowed. At no time shall a physician perform non-traditional procedures. Examples of non-traditional procedures include chiropractic procedures, acupuncture or spiritual healing. 6. If, at any time there is a problem or discrepancy in procedures, OFD EMS personnel may contact the receiving hospital by phone or radio and have the receiving hospital physician speak directly to the physician at the scene. “DO NOT RESUSCITATE” (DNR) ORDERS AND IDENTIFICATION OF CPR ONLY (Adopted from the “Nebraska EMS Model Protocols”) With the advent of individuals taking greater responsibility for their own health care, decisions made by the individuals themselves, to not prolong their own life, is recognized as valid and has become more common. Health Care Power of Attorney (HCPA), Living Wills and Do Not Resuscitate (DNR) orders are encountered frequently by OFD personnel. Occasionally, a family member will call 911 even when a DNR order exists or a Living Will is present. There are also times when OFD will arrive on scene, begin resuscitation procedures and a DNR or Living Will is discovered. This protocol is designed to provide guidance for such situations. Do Not Resuscitate (DNR) - A DNR is a written order by a physician that a patient should not be resuscitated or have CPR performed. A DNR must be signed by a physician, dated and have the patient’s name on it. OFD EMS personnel can honor a DNR. The OFD EMS provider must be identified in the patient care report. Verbal confirmation of a DNR by a family member or friend without verification of a written DNR is not sufficient to withhold resuscitation efforts. Health Care Power of Attorney (HCPA) – is a legal document stating the name of the person the individual (patient) has named as the person who will make medical decisions for their care. It should be signed by the patient and the patient’s attorney, and only applies to adults. It is “executed” only when the patient is unable to consent by reason of advanced illness. As long as the patient is of sound mind, the patient is still the final authority on what may be performed on them. The HCPA is the final authority once the patient’s illness has reached the stage where the patient is no longer capable of making informed consent. Living Will – This document states the patient’s wishes should they require resuscitation or life support measures. The document must be signed by the patient and the patient’s physician and only applies to adults. OFD EMS providers will not initiate or continue cardiopulmonary resuscitation on a patient in cardiac arrest once a valid DNR order is confirmed. In the event of uncertainty, resuscitative measures should be initiated.
DNR does not mean that emergency medical care for any other medical condition will be changed or limited. Patients should receive emergency medical treatment (BVM / airway management, IV therapy and pharmacology) up until the point of cardiac arrest. Physicians may designate a patient as DNR by written order, verbally (when the physician is physically present at the scene), or by telephone consult from the paramedic on scene to the patient’s physician. A written DNR order must contain the patient’s name and be signed by the physician or by the RN who received the order from the physician. If a physician physically present at the scene designates the patient as a DNR, the paramedic shall ask the physician to document the DNR designation on the OFD 179 or ePCR and sign it. If the physician refuses to document and sign the OFD 179 or ePCR, the paramedic shall initiate resuscitative measures. In a skilled care facility (nursing home), DNR orders documented in the patient’s medical record are considered valid if signed by the physician or by the RN for the physician. A DNR form may be used, but is not required in the nursing home setting. The OFD EMS provider who observed the DNR order must be identified on the patient care report. An OFD EMS provider can honor an effective Living Will or Health Care Power of Attorney. This must be directly observed. OFD EMS providers can presume the validity of either of these documents if signed in Nebraska. Documents from other states in compliance with that state’s laws are also valid in Nebraska. If the family desires CPR and/or resuscitation in the presence of a DNR or HCPA, the family’s wishes shall be honored. Observation of an original or a photocopy of a living will or health care power of attorney must be documented in the patient care report. An OFD EMS provider shall not honor a living will if there is no information or evidence that a physician has determined the patient is in a terminal condition or in a persistent vegetative state. If there is information or evidence that a physician has determined the patient is in a terminal condition or in a persistent vegetative state, this information should be documented in the patient care report. The patient care report must also contain information that the patient is an adult (is 19 or older or has been married). If a telephone consult with the patient’s physician or the physician’s designee verifies a DNR, the OFD paramedic can honor the order. Authorization shall be documented on the patient care report and include the physician’s or physician designee’s name, telephone number and time of the telephone call from the paramedic to the physician. Once CPR has been initiated, resuscitative measures may be discontinued when any one of the following occurs:    A DNR or no code order is confirmed
   A living will or HCPA for an adult is being followed    A physician physically present at the scene or medical control for the service, based on information from the out-of-hospital providers on scene, determines that CPR is futile or should be discontinued    An OFD EMS provider is following termination of CPR protocols that have been authorized by the Physician Medical Director    Any time the scene becomes unsafe for rescuers    When signs of late death or all the signs of early death are identified, the OFD paramedic may discontinue CPR if previously started (see Code 4 protocol). The paramedic will document a one minute printed rhythm strip from the cardiac monitor if no signs of late death are apparent. Management of Home Hospice Patient Attempts should be made to contact the hospice representative to provide additional guidance and support to the family prior to transporting. If the family desires CPR and/or resuscitation in the presence of a DNR or HCPA, the family’s wishes shall be honored. Resuscitation of a DNR Patient If inadvertently, a DNR patient is resuscitated, and in the absence of physician directives, care should be continued and the patient should be taken to the catchment area hospital. Notification to OFD Dispatch Once resuscitative measures are terminated, notify OFD dispatch and request an OPD cruiser if one has not already been dispatched. CODE 4 PATIENTS OFD personnel may be called to a scene where biologic and clinical death is evident. In such cases, it is not necessary to begin resuscitation. Situations may also occur where CPR has been initiated on an obviously deceased patient prior to the arrival of OFD EMS personnel. This protocol is intended to assist OFD personnel in the identification of such situations. If the patient meets code 4 criteria, OFD EMS providers may discontinue CPR or may choose not to initiate CPR. A Code 4 patient is identified if he/she is described by one of the following categories: 1. Patient with obvious lethal injury – trauma cardiac arrest with injuries incompatible with life (i.e. massive blood loss, displacement of brain tissue, decapitation) 2. Patient with one or more of the signs of late death:
a. Wrinkled cornea b. Rigor mortis c. Postmortem lividity d. Decomposition Paramedic shall obtain and document a 1 minute ECG rhythm strip demonstrating asystole. 3. Patient with all the following signs of early death: a. Unresponsive to all stimuli b. No pulse c. No spontaneous respirations d. Pupils which are fixed and dilated Paramedic shall obtain and document a 1 minute ECG rhythm strip demonstrating asystole. 4. Valid DNR form 5. Physician authorization 6. No patient will be declared Code 4 without a complete hands-on physical evaluation including: a. No pulse, respiration or blood pressure b. No response to painful stimuli, no corneal reflex, and no response to a sternal rub c. All of the above must be documented in the patient care report d. The purpose of this protocol is to reduce EMS provider risk while rapidly forward deploying medical resources and to assist in treatment, stabilization, and evacuation of the wounded despite hazardous conditions that might otherwise delay treatment. DEFINITIONS Hot Zone: Any area in the area of operations in which there is a direct and immediate threat to persons or providers. This may be described as an area that has not been cleared by Law Enforcement, or an area in which there is active engagement with a direct threat. Warm Zone: Any area in the area of operations where there is a potential hostile threat to persons or providers but is not direct and immediate. This may be the main zone of operations and staging for EMS providers. (An incident within a large building may require that an interior casualty collection point be located in the Warm Zone due the compartmentalized nature of the scene.) Cold Zone: Any area within the area of operations where the Unified Incident Commanders do not reasonably anticipate a significant danger or threat to the providers or patients. Exterior casualty collection points, Command assets, staged non-tactical Fire/EMS personnel and all apparatus are located will be located in the Cold Zone. Active Shooter: Any armed person who uses or has used deadly physical force on other persons and continues to do so while having unrestricted access to additional victims. Contact Team: Initial teams of LEOs who form immediately on arrival to the scene of an active shooter and immediately deploy into a building moving rapidly with the goal of initiating contact to contain/eliminate the active shooter to prevent further injury or loss of life. Rescue Task Force (RTF): Teams formed upon the request of the initial contact teams for the purpose of initial triage, correcting any life-threatening injuries to victims found, and pulling victims out of the identified warm zone into the cold zone for further triage, treatment and transport.

Any patient with known or suspected thoracic injury, and any respiratory distress either initially or that develops during care, will have a prophylactic needle decompression upon arrival at the CCP.

Through a collaboration of several public safety entities and youth services programs, the program will educate and prepare the youth with the tools needed to become a fire safety advocate. PROGRAM The Omaha Fire Department has a YFI program divided into three age groups using the National Fire Academy curriculum that is used across the country. The age groups are 2-7, 8-11 and 12- 18. The curriculum used for these three age groups are age appropriate. Alterations may be made to each program to fit the needs of each individual child. The YFI program is part of the Fire Prevention and Education Division and is managed by the YFI Coordinator. The YFI Coordinator will manage all aspects of the program and report to the Chief of the Fire Prevention and Education Division. Types of Juvenile Fire Setters Curiosity / Experimentation Fire Setters are children who act out of curiosity and do not understand the consequences of their behavior. Usually, there is an attempt by the juvenile to extinguish the fire or call for help. They will have feelings of guilt or remorse after the incident. Fire safety education and education about the consequences of playing with fire are effective when dealing with this type of fire starter. Crisis or Troubled Fire Setters are juveniles calling attention to a problem they might have. The youth may have poor coping / problem-solving skills. FIRE PREVENTION & EDUCATION 3-1 SPECIAL FIRE PERMITS FOR CEREMONIAL FIRES, BONFIRES, ETC. REVISION HISTORY REVISION #   REVISION DESCRIPTION     DATE REVISED        AUTHOR          REVIEWED BY     APPROVED BY 1              New format            Nov 2015        McCaw 897        McCormick 638    Kanger 601 2             Burn permits           April 2021     Fitzpatrick 885    McCaw 897        Olsen 640 PURPOSE To provide guidelines for special fire permits that are issued. POLICY These guidelines apply only to ceremonial fires, bonfires, or open-fires employed by schools, boy scouts, and similar groups whose primary purpose for an open fire is for the visual appreciation of such groups. These guidelines are not intended to grant permission to conduct open burning of any other nature. All burn permits will be issued by the Assistant Fire Marshal (AFM) for burning conducted outside the City limits but within OFD jurisdiction. Location Ceremonial fires, bonfires, etc., shall not be located within fifty (50) feet of any combustible structure. Due consideration shall be given to overhead wiring and similar exposures when selecting a burning site. Fires will be permitted only in areas that are accessible to Fire Department apparatus. A Fire Department representative must inspect the site beforehand. Size or Area of Fire The Chief of the Fire Department, the Chief of the Fire Prevention Division, or his duly authorized agent, shall determine the allowable size of any pile of combustible material that is to be burned. However, in no case shall the pile exceed six (6) feet by six (6) feet by four (4) feet in height. There shall not be more than one such pile permitted at any one site. What Can Be Burned Only "clean" or free burning combustible material such as dry kindling wood and scrap lumber, shall be burned. No roofing material, plastics, overstuffed furniture, mattresses or any substance that gives off copious amounts of virulent smoke shall be burned. Due to the fly particles that develop when burned, the use of newspaper, paper, cardboard and similar material is limited to that required to "Start" the fire. Adult Attendant Required At least one adult shall be in direct attendance at every fire site and when good judgement dictates, as many attendants as deemed by the Chief of the Department, the Chief of the Fire Prevention Division, or his duly authorized agent, shall be provided.

--- FPE 3-1 ---
Use of Fuels to Start Fire No flammable or combustible liquids shall be used as a "starter" or a "propellant" for any fire coming within the scope of these guidelines. Fire Watch It is the responsibility of the party obtaining the permit to establish a fire watch and to make sure the fire is completely extinguished with water before he or she leaves the property. Special Fire Permit Required Applications for a special Fire Permit must be obtained from the Air Quality Control Section of the Permits and Inspection Division of the City Planning Department. If these applications are approved by Air Quality Control, they are forwarded to the Fire Prevention Division (FPD). The proposed fire site is then inspected by the Fire Prevention Division. If the above guidelines are met, the permit is issued to the applicant. Burn Permits for Areas Outside City Limits The AFM will authorize burn permits that are outside city limits but within OFD response territory and jurisdiction. For burn permits in the Elkhorn Suburban Fire District, the Elkhorn Suburban Fire Chief may issue a burn permit. This burn permit will then be sent to the OFD AFM’s office to keep on file. The Burn Permit can be issued for up to a 3 month time frame. Revocation of Special Fire Permits Violation of any of the provisions in these guidelines may result in the revocation of the Special Fire Permit issued. The Chief of the Fire Department reserves the right to cancel any permit due to changes in climatic conditions such as tinder dry periods, windy days, etc., between the date when the permit is applied for and the actual time and date of the fire. Prohibited Open Burning Open burning that will be offensive or objectionable because of smoke or odor emissions when atmospheric conditions or local circumstances make such fires hazardous shall be prohibited. The fire code official or the fire department is authorized to order the extinguishment by the permit holder of open burning which creates or adds to a hazardous or objectionable situation. A citation may be issued by Law Enforcement if the fire is deemed illegal by an OFD Officer. First offense warnings may be allowed and educating of the responsible party is advised.
