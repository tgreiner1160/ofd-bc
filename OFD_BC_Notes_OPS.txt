BACKING FIRE DEPARTMENT APPARATUS AND VEHICLES Avoid backing when possible. Where backing is unavoidable, use guides to direct traffic and/or assist the driver in avoiding accidents. Any time an apparatus must be backed, a guide is required* to direct the driver. To avoid confusion, only one crewmember should be utilized as guide and be located on the left rear. Other crewmembers should monitor traffic and other unsafe conditions that the driver or guide is unable to see; height clearance, front basket or ladder over hang clearance, etc. If needed, place a crewmember on the right front of the vehicles so all sides can then be monitored. Note* in an emergency situation when guides are unavailable, the driver shall dismount and walk completely around the apparatus before backing. The guide shall always be in the driver’s view through one of the side mirrors. Once the driver and the guide have determined the direction of travel, the guide shall determine and control the movement of the apparatus. If there is doubt as to the safety of either the guide or apparatus, the driver shall stop the apparatus until the proper adjustments are made. Emergency vehicles that have responded to an incident shall seek assistance from a credible individual(s) to assist in backing the Fire Department vehicle:  Other fire personnel  Police officer  Credible bystander Fire Department vehicles backing into the fire station bay shall have a firefighter/paramedic posted at the rear of the vehicle and in view of the driver’s left side mirror. Signals for backing up Straight Back: One hand above the head with palm toward face, waving back. Other hand at your side. (Left or right hand optional.) Turn:           Both arms pointing the same direction with index fingers extended. (The driver shall determine the direction of travel, the guide shall control of the movement of the apparatus.) Stop:           Both arms thrust above head with open hands toward driver. Night Backing: Signals will be the same. The guide will assure that the spotlights on the rear of the apparatus are turned on before he/she allows the apparatus to be backed. A flashlight is carried, but at no time will it be directed toward the mirror.
Backing Up to Wooden Stop Block If an apparatus backs up far enough to come in contact with or run over a wooden stop block, there is a danger of the block becoming a projectile due to the pressure placed upon it from the weight of the apparatus. Therefore, apparatus drivers and personnel acting as guides shall avoid having apparatus wheels come in contact with the wooden stop blocks. While the wooden stop blocks shall be kept in place as a back-up to the guide to ensure that an apparatus does not back up too far, the wooden blocks shall be placed so that they are a few inches behind where the apparatus’ back wheels normally stop. The person guiding the apparatus back shall ensure that the apparatus is stopped just before the wheels contact the wooden stop blocks. In all cases, apparatus drivers and their guides shall ensure that the apparatus is backed in a slow, controlled and safe manner. When one wheel is slipping: The primary purpose is to provide power to both rear wheels equally when one is on a low-traction surface. When to avoid using a rear axle lock: On dry pavement: Never use a rear axle lock on dry, paved surfaces, as it increases tire wear, creates noise, and can cause significant damage to the drivetrain.
While turning: Do not engage the lock when turning on pavement because the outer wheel needs to travel a greater distance than the inner wheel. This difference is impossible with a locked differential, causing bind-up and stress on the axle and tires. At high speeds: Rear axle locks are for slow-speed off-road use. Driving at high speeds with the lock engaged is dangerous and increases the shock load on the drivetrain. On steep side slopes: A locked axle can make it difficult to steer and control the vehicle, potentially causing it to slide downhill. During heavy acceleration: Using the lock during sudden, heavy acceleration puts extreme stress on the drivetrain components, which can lead to failure. When already stuck due to other factors: If the vehicle is wedged or stuck for a reason other than a lack of traction on all wheels, engaging the lock may not help and could cause damage as the wheels spin against the obstruction. To engage the differential lock:  Slow down, reduce your speed, and come to a stop. Do not engage while wheels are spinning, as this can cause damage.  Release the accelerator, which is crucial to relieve torque on the gears and allo w the lock to engage properly.    Engage the switch by moving the dash-mounted switch to the "Lock" position.    Confirm engagement: An indicator light will confirm the lock is engaged.    Slowly accelerate out of the low traction situation.    Try to move in as straight a direction as possible. Steering will be adversely affected, as an understeer condition will be created by both sides of the rear axle moving at the same speed. This also places increased forces on the drivetrain and rear end components, which could lead to damage.  Once you have cleared the low traction situation the differential lock must be disengaged. To disengage the rear wheel differential lock:  Slow down, reduce your speed, and come to a stop.  Momentarily release the accelerator, which is crucial to relieve torque on the gears and allow the lock to disengage properly.    Flip the switch back to the "unlock" or "off" position.    Confirm disengagement: The indicator will turn off.    Slowly accelerate and make sure the differential lock has disengaged.    Resume normal driving.

OPERATIONS 4-0 FIRE CONTROL REVISION HISTORY REVISION #    REVISION DESCRIPTION      DATE REVISED        AUTHOR        REVIEWED BY       APPROVED BY 1             Periodic Revision        Oct 2015       Langlois 888     Dunford 633        Hunter 723 2             Periodic Revision        Dec 2025      Landholm 1242   McLaughlin 1035     Bossman 729 PURPOSE Effective fireground operations begin with the selection of an appropriate fire suppression strategy, offensive or defensive, based on a dynamic risk assessment, size-up findings, and incident priorities. The chosen strategy dictates the overall approach to fire attack, resource deployment, and firefighter positioning. An offensive strategy prioritizes rapidly occupying a space using operations to locate and extinguish the fire at its seat, often when there is a chance to save lives or property. Conversely, a defensive strategy is implemented when conditions pose an unacceptable risk to personnel, and efforts shift toward exposure protection and perimeter control from the exterior. Determining the correct strategy requires evaluating structural integrity, fire behavior, signs of occupancy or life, and available resources to ensure actions are aligned with life safety, incident stabilization, and property conservation objectives. OFFENSIVE STRATEGY The goal of an offensive strategy fire attack is to rapidly locate, contain, and suppress the fire while prioritizing life safety, incident stabilization, and property conservation. This approach relies on a thorough size-up, identification of fire location, and an understanding of fire dynamics. Crews operating offensively should apply water to the fire compartment as soon as possible to rapidly reduce interior temperatures. Exterior Offensive Fire Attack An Exterior Offensive Fire Attack involves applying water to the fire compartment from outside (such as through a window or door) to rapidly cool the interior and improve conditions before firefighters enter. •    Reduces Interior Temperature and Heat Release Rate Exterior water application, when directed into the fire compartment, rapidly reduces the temperature and heat release rate (HHR), suppressing flames and improving interior conditions in both the fire room and adjacent spaces.

•   Improves Tenability for Potential Occupants Cooling the compartment lowers toxic gas concentrations and ambient temperatures, enhancing the survivability profile for any occupants who may still be inside. •   Provides a Tactical Time Gain for Interior Operations By immediately disrupting the fire's energy, exterior suppression creates a more controlled environment, giving interior crews improved conditions for search, rescue, and fire attack. •   Does Not Adversely Impact Flow Path When Applied Correctly FSRI research demonstrates that early, well-placed exterior streams do not push fire or hot gases deeper into the structure. Instead, they interrupt the fire's progression and help stabilize the flow path when coordinated with interior tactics. Exterior Offensive Fire Attack Considerations •   Location of Stream Placement Deflection angles – a steeper angle results in more surface coverage •   Use of 2 lines One interior – one exterior •   Minimalize Air Entrainment Air entrainment in hose streams is less dependent on the flow and nozzle pressure and more dependent on the movement and speed of the pattern chosen •   Stream Pattern Choice Solid or straight streams maintain large enough droplets to ensure the most amount of heat is absorbed and most surfaces are cooled and minimizes air entrainment. Fog patterns introduce in excess of 15,000 cubic feet of air, similar to that of a PPV fan. Although it can be effective at cooling the gas layer, the air entrainment causes an adverse global effect. •   Duration of flow Observe changes in smoke color, volume, and velocity post-application – gas contraction. If no improvement is noted, reassess fire location, stream placement, or strategy. A crew may initiate fire suppression by applying water from the exterior to reduce heat and improve interior conditions followed by an interior push. Door control should be used to limit air entrainment and restrict fire growth, creating a safer environment for interior operations.
Interior Offensive Fire Attack An interior offensive attack, as described by the Fire Safety Research Institute (FSRI), is a fire suppression strategy where firefighters enter the structure with the primary goal of applying water directly to the seat of the fire to quickly reduce the heat release rate (HRR), limit fire growth, and improve tenability conditions for both occupants and responders. This strategy is typically employed when conditions indicate that the structure is safe to enter, there is a potential for savable lives or property, and the fire can be controlled through direct interior action. It emphasizes rapid water application guided by an understanding of fire dynamics. According to FSRI research, success in an interior offensive attack is directly linked to: •   Applying water quickly to the burning surfaces to reduce temperature and gas-phase fuel production •   Managing openings to control air flow (e.g., door control), •   Using coordinated tactics that consider both firefighter and occupant survivability profiles. Interior Offensive Attack Considerations •   Flow and Go Use an “O” or inverted “U” pattern, wall-ceiling-wall, while advancing to the fire compartment. Deflect water droplets to fire compartment prior to entry. Cooling the upper gas layer disrupts convection currents and reduces the risk of flashover. Do not move past fire to get to the seat of the fire. •   Minimalize Air Entrainment Air entrainment in hose streams is less dependent on the flow and nozzle pressure and more dependent on the movement and speed of the pattern chosen. •   Stream Pattern Choice Straight or solid streams maintain large enough droplets of water to ensure the most amount of heat is absorbed and the most amount of surfaces are cooled. Using a fog stream may be effective at cooling the gas layer, but the size of the droplets do not allow for sufficient surface cooling. With minimal surface cooling and maximum air entrainment, fog patterns are not an effective choice for fire knockdown and suppression. OFFENSIVE ATTACK RESPONSIBILITIES When committing to interior offensive fire attack:
•   Conduct an Informed Size-Up o Determine fire location, size, behavior, and growth potential. o Anticipate the fire’s path and make decisions that keep crews ahead of it. o Can use B.A.G. Acronym: "Been, At, Going" •   Control All Sides o Address all seven sides: front, rear, both sides, top, bottom, and interior. •   Control the Flow Path o Identify and manage air movement. o Any exterior opening should be thought of as ventilation o Compartment isolation is a form of fire suppression. If the fire compartment is located prior to fire suppression, the compartment should be isolated to prevent rapid fire growth. o Door Control •   Ensure Effective Water Application o Deploy an attack line capable of exceeding the fire's Heat Release Rate (HRR). Use OFD resources accordingly: o 1¾” line, flowing 150 GPM, for residential and smaller fires. o 2½” line, Flowing 260 GPM, for high-volume fire conditions, especially in: • Advanced fire conditions • Commercial occupancies • Unknown fire location or extent • Large, uncompartmentalized spaces • High water demand (GPM vs. BTUs) • Standpipe operations •   Put Water on the Fire o Prioritize fast, direct water application to the seat of the fire. o Coordinated ventilation must be timed with suppression and based on fire location and behavior. o Open concealed spaces early (attics, voids) and apply water to the compartment o Utilize the principles of water mapping for maximum surface coverage •   Establish an Exit Strategy o Pre-plan primary and secondary egress routes. o Ensure all interior crews are aware of emergency exits and that they are maintained throughout the operation. •   Protect Exposures o Prioritize protecting exposures in the most threatened direction of fire spread.
Effective fireground leadership starts with Command recognizing conditions, forecasting fire behavior, and matching tactics to the fire development. Offensive operations are aggressive but risk-managed and data-driven. DEFENSIVE STRATEGY A defensive strategy is used when interior attack is too dangerous—operations are conducted from the exterior. Tactics used: o Master streams, aerials, deck guns o Exposure protection o Perimeter control Conditions: o Structural instability o Fire too large or unsafe for interior crews o No viable victims or confirmed all-clear When switching from offensive mode to defensive mode, all interior personnel will exit following OPS 10-2 (Emergency Evacuation or OPS 10-3 (Orderly Withdrawal), based on urgency. After exiting, maintain a safe perimeter. Captains must account for their crews (PAR) and report to Command. Command must have confirmation that all interior personnel are out and accounted for before beginning defensive operations. Upon switching to defensive mode, interior lines will be removed or, if necessary abandoned, then repositioned. Identify and protect all immediate and potential exposures. Master streams may be considered a primary tactic for defensive operations. “Fire Under Control” is the standard radio report used when forward fire progress has been stopped and remaining fire can be handled with on-scene resources. It does not mean the fire is fully out. Command must transmit this report to Dispatch, who will log the time.

Command must structure initial operations around the completion of the search function. PRIMARY SEARCH: A quick search through all affected areas that are able to be accessed to find and remove, or verify the removal and/or safety of all occupants. Time is the critical factor in the primary search process. Successful primary search operations must necessarily be extended quickly and during initial fire stages. SECONDARY SEARCH: The search function that is conducted once the fire is out or at least well under control. Secondary search means that companies thoroughly search the interior of the fire area after initial fire control and ventilation activities have been completed. Secondary search should preferably be completed by different companies than those involved in primary search activities. Thoroughness, rather than time, is the critical factor in secondary search. The completion of the primary search is reported utilizing the standard radio reporting term "ALL CLEAR." It is the responsibility of Command to coordinate primary search assignments, secure completion reports from interior companies, and to transmit the "ALL CLEAR" report to Dispatch. Dispatch will record the time of this report from Command. Part of the initial size-up should include determining a rescue profile. The rescue profile helps to determine the probability, location, and status of potential victims as well as the avenues of access and egress. Rescue profile decision-making should focus on the probability of the existence of a savable life. Factors that should be evaluated to determine the rescue profile include, but are not limited to:     Occupancy type

COMMAND MUST CONSIDER THE FOLLOWING factors in developing a basic rescue size- up:   Number, location and condition of victims   Affect the fire has on the victims   Capability of the control forces to enter the building, remove/protect victims and control fire There are two ways to conduct rescue of victims: 1. Remove victims from fire 2. Remove the fire from the victims Often the most effective life-saving effort is to advance a hose line to attack, confine, and extinguish the fire. This may include protecting egress paths for trapped victims as well as eliminating the danger of the fire itself. Command must realistically evaluate the manpower required to actually remove victims and then treat their fire-affected bodies. In cases involving such multiple victims, Command must call for the timely response of adequate resource and quickly develop an organization that will both stabilize the fire and provide for the removal and treatment of the occupants.
Rescue efforts should be extended in the following order:    Most severely threatened    The largest number (groups)    The remainder of the fire area    The exposed areas Command must make specific primary search assignments to companies to cover specific areas of large, complex occupancies and maintain on-going control of such companies until the entire area is searched. Multiple single resources assigned to search should be coordinated by a Search Group Supervisor or assigned to a Division Supervisor within a certain geographic area. When primary search companies encounter and remove victims, Command must assign other companies to continue to cover the interior positions vacated by those companies. All initial attack efforts must be directed toward supporting rescue efforts and hose lines must be placed in a manner to control interior access, confine the fire, and protect avenues of escape. Hose line placement becomes a critical factor in these cases and Command, as well as all operating companies must realize that the operation is in a rescue mode. It may be necessary to operate in a manner that writes off the structure in order to buy rescue time. Normal means of interior access (stairs, halls, interior public areas, etc.) should be utilized to remove victims whenever possible. Secondary means of rescue (platforms, ladders, fire escapes, helicopters, gravity/nets, etc.) must be utilized in order of effec tiveness. Command must structure treatment of victims after removal. Multiple victims should be removed to one location for more effective triage and treatment. Command should coordinate and utilize Paramedic and EMT-Basic capability wherever available and assign treatment companies as required to a Medical Group. Once the primary search has been completed and an "ALL CLEAR" transmitted, Command must maintain control of access to the fire area; beware of occupants (and others) re -entering the building. The most urgent reason for calling additional alarms is for the purpose of covering life safety – Command must develop a realistic (and pessimistic) rescue size up as early as possible.

DEFINITIONS: Hazardous/IDLH atmosphere: Any atmosphere in which OFD firefighters are required to use SCBA as defined by SWD 1-0 Protective Equipment/Clothing. Air Management: An ongoing assessment of air consumption by individual firefighters and/or Division/Group supervisors who are breathing air from SCBA. Firefighters in hazardous/IDLH atmospheres must regularly check their pressure gauges to know how much air they have left in their bottle. The Rule of Air Management (R.O.A.M.): Know how much air you have used, and manage the amount of air you have left in your bottle so that you leave the hazardous atmosphere before your SCBA’s low-air alarm activates. Point of No Return: Refers to the firefighter’s individual management of his/her air supply. It is determined by the amount of air consumed going into a hazardous atmosphere versus the amount of residual air needed to safely exit the atmosphere. AIR MANAGEMENT POLICY It is the expectation that all OFD firefighters using SCBA in accordance with SWD 1-0 shall comply with the following:     Check their air levels before entering a hazardous atmosphere. Members must have a minimum of 4000psi (As per GO RDS 2007-03) in their cylinder in order to make entry into a hazardous atmosphere.     Follow the Rule of Air Management when operating in any hazardous/IDLH atmosphere.

RADIO COMMUNICATIONS All operations of the radio system shall comply with the regulations of the Federal Communications Commission (FCC), Part 90 - Rules Governing Public Safety Radio Services. The dispatcher at the fire console in the 911 Center shall be responsible for clearing air traffic as quickly as possible, for maintaining discipline on the air and for determining the order of priority on simultaneous transmissions. His/her orders shall be strictly obeyed. All conversations shall be as brief and concise as possible and all unnecessary repetition avoided. During extremely busy periods (multiple alarms, multi-casualty incidents, wide- spread storms, etc.) maintain strict radio discipline, prioritize transmissions, and stay off the radio as much as possible. In transmitting on both mobile and portable radios, holding the microphone one to two inches from the mouth and at a 45 degree angle is important for a clear transmission. Speak across the microphone instead of directly into it. Speak clearly in a normal conversational tone. DO NOT SHOUT. All radio conversations shall be business-like without personal greetings or pleasantries. No personal communications are allowed, such as “Jim, tell John to call his wife.” All operators of the radio system shall be courteous, but expressions of courtesy such as “thanks” and “please” shall be avoided in the interest of brevity. Indecent, obscene or profane language is expressly prohibited. Any member of this Department in a radio equipped vehicle and outside the fire station shall always keep the radio turned on. The exception to this rule shall be when any Chief’s car is at the scene of a fire and there are other engine companies present, such Chief’s car radio may be turned off to save the battery/fuel. The portable radio or engine compa ny radio shall be used if needed. Other department passenger and shop vehicles are also excepted when occupants are away from the vehicle. Be sure to have the vehicle engine running when using the mobile radio for transmitting. Transmitting can drain your vehicle battery over time. Monitoring-only will not require as much current flow. All electrical systems add to drainage of the vehicle battery, and therefore running the engine to maintain battery life should be considered. Any firefighter while on a radio-equipped vehicle, regardless of rank, shall promptly carry out any radio-transmitted order. There shall be NO disputes or arguments of any kind over the radio system. Remember that the public and news media monitor our communications and our actions are public. No tampering - No person shall tamper with radio equipment or make any changes whatsoever in the radio equipment other than the normal adjustments (i.e.; volume and channel/talkgroup changing).
Use of good judgment - Members of the Omaha Fire Department should understand that these rules and regulations are not expected to cover each and every specific situation that may arise in the discharge of their duties, but that something must necessarily be left to the intelligence, discretion and good judgment of the members. Responsibility of Command Officers for Communications It shall be the responsibility of ALL Commanding Officers to enforce these procedures and see that personnel under their command constantly follow them. Radio Transmissions (Communications Model) When making a radio transmission, the person transmitting announces the call sign of the unit they are calling first and then their own call sign: Examples           #1: Engine 24 is trying to call Truck 34 (E-24) - “Truck 34 FROM Engine 24” #2: The Incident Commander is trying to call Fire Attack (IC) - “Fire Attack FROM Command” LISTEN before transmitting. If there is radio traffic on your selected channel and the PTT button is pushed, you will hear a solid low-pitched tone (BONK), indicating the channel is busy. Wait until traffic clears before pressing the PTT button. The system will not allow one radio user to talk over or cover another. Repeating Transmissions (Feedback loop) When units/personnel receive information/orders via the radio, they shall acknowledge the information received by repeating a brief version of what was received, especially when receiving dispatch information, such as for EMS incidents. Move Ups The zone designated by the fire dispatcher will govern all multiple alarm move ups . Upon notification that a second alarm or greater has been dispatched, fire companies shall consult their Fire Zone book and move up accordingly. Whenever a company moves-up to the quarters or territory of another company, such move-up company will continue to use its own assigned company number and will assume responsibility of that territory into which it has moved, unless dispatched otherwise. On move-ups, all companies will move-up according to zone cards without sirens or speed. As w/ 2nd alarms and greater, the zone cards will indicate any required move- ups by zone. Dispatch will also call the affected move-up companies by phone or radio as needed. All other rules regarding move-ups or move-backs apply here. Move Back Whenever a regular assigned company or extra rig is reported "in-service" in quarters or territory occupied by a move-up company, such move-up company may be ordered by the dispatcher to move-back in reverse order as listed in the zone book, or the company may be moved into other quarters. Companies and units must not move back without the dispatcher's knowledge. Expedite Status/Help A Firefighter On occasion, Omaha Fire Department personnel, while answering medic or fire alarm calls, have been threatened by hostile parties and prevented from administering emergency assistance to a sick or injured person.
When a company or medic unit is threatened with bodily harm, they can request that dispatch “EXPEDITE” the police. The police will respond as fast as they can, using red lights and siren. When a company or medic unit is being assaulted, ask the dispatcher for a "HELP A FIREFIGHTER." If personnel are not able tell dispatch of their situation, an emergency can be initiated by pushing the Orange Emergency Button (OEB) and the PTT button. In this case, the dispatcher will call back asking if personnel are “Signal 88”. If personnel respond in the negative (“No, I am not Signal 88.”) or if there is no response, two police cruisers and a police supervisor will be dispatched on an expedite basis and respond using red lights and sirens. A firefighter can also notify dispatch of a “Help a firefighter” situation by activating the Orange Emergency Button (OEB) on their radio and pushing the PTT button. Dispatch will receive audible and visual signals at their console. In addition, the Battalion Chief for the unit requesting "Help a Firefighter", a Medic Unit and the Shift Supervisor will be dispatched to the scene on a Special Duty. The company or medic officer making the request shall write a letter to the Chief of the Department explaining the circumstances of the event. NOTE: The request to “Help a Firefighter" shall not be used indiscriminately. Company Officers should use good judgment when assessing a scene for safety. If there is a danger that firefighters may become victims, the scene should not be entered until secured by police. If fire personnel are already on the scene when it becomes dangerous, patients should be loaded into the medic unit as quickly as possible and all personnel should leave the scene. Patients can be assessed and treated away from the scene.

OPERATIONS 10-1 MAYDAY PROCEDURES REVISION HISTORY REVISION #   REVISION DESCRIPTION       DATE REVISED         AUTHOR         REVIEWED BY       APPROVED BY 1            Periodic Review           Nov 2015        Kleffner 696 & McEvoy 680      McCormick 638 Andersen 622 Barney 670 &     McCormick 638 & 2            Radio Upgrades            Dec 2017                                              Olsen 640 Folger 783        McCaw 897 PURPOSE The Mayday is a firefighter’s call for help. It is a firefighter’s last effort to save their own life from a hazardous/IDLH (Immediately Dangerous to Life and Health) environment. The following policy will replace the way the Omaha Fire Department currently performs Mayday operations. The most important goal of the Mayday procedure is to not only simplify the process in which the firefighter(s) call for a Mayday, but to also simplify and streamline the command procedures of the Omaha Fire Department and Douglas County 911 during these difficult events. The Firefighter may initiate a Mayday anytime they feel they are presented with a life - threatening condition. If in doubt, call the Mayday. MAYDAY PROCEDURES Calling a Mayday Step One (1): Verbally Broadcast a Mayday Immediately key your radio (on the originally assigned TAC channel) and announce Mayday three times on the assigned TAC channel, A1 or whichever DC911 assigned at dispatch. The Mayday message should include appropriate information to address the following areas:  Who: Name, unit ID, position assignment, etc.  What: Your Situation, what’s wrong, what you need help with, etc.  Where: Your location as descriptive as possible. The following is an example of a Mayday message for a firefighter who was assigned to Search and fell through the floor into the basement: Firefighter: MAYDAY, MAYDAY, MAYDAY (announcement) - This is FF Smith, E-1 (who), I just fell through Division 1’s floor (what), I am in the basement in the A-B Corner (where).

POLICY All wires and equipment are to be considered energized and that they may cause serious bodily harm. If the status of a piece of equipment or wire is unknown, it shall be considered live until proven otherwise. Procedures within this SOP shall be regarded as policy to keep personnel safe. When wires are found down, or poles are found damaged by fire personnel, OPPD shall be notified through Fire Dispatch. If a pole is damaged, advise Dispatch if it is a metal or wooden pole. Storm damage may force wires down over a large area. In this case, Dispatch, along with FC2 will make the determination on a change in response procedures. When responding to incidents with “downed”, loose, or sagging wires, secondary collapse of poles and towers are common up and down the line. Apparatus shall be parked a minimum of one pole-span from the trouble area, as the spans adjacent to the trouble may have been weakened and may be under severe tension. Treat all wires as energized until notified otherwise by a “QUALIFIED” OPPD representative. Personnel should use caution when exiting the apparatus so that no contact is made with an energized wire. Move all personnel or bystanders back from the danger zone (at least one pole-span each direction). Any movement of the wires in trouble, caused by the wind, rescue work, or other cause, may cause other wires to burn down. Fire Department personnel are not to leave the scene where wires are down until properly relieved by OPPD and the wires are clear and safe. All wires, regardless of their location on the pole, should be considered live wires. When responding to incidents of “downed” wires or where the potential for downed wires exists, caution should be used around metal gates and wire fences as they may be energized from contact with the wire, even if it is some distance away. See Figure 1. Wire is sold on reels or in coils. Often it retains some of its original coil even after it has been installed. If a wire drops to the ground and must be cleared of a victim, there is always the possibility of its Figure 1: Down wire curling up and running along the ground creating a hazard for MINIMUM standoff distance. bystanders. The wire may also slack back toward the pole and contact other overhead wires, which in turn may be burned down, causing further dangers.
VICTIM RESCUE When a victim or vehicle is in contact with electrical wires, the following precautions shall be taken:    Immediately notify OPPD    Do not, under any circumstances, permit your body or clothing to touch the victim or vehicle. No one can tell from the appearance of a wire whether or not it is energized. Wires on the ground may be energized and still not be sparking. Supposed “dead” wires are not necessarily safe wires. Sometimes they may be re-energized as a result of induced voltage from parallel lines, contact with some other energized wire, from feedback through connected load, or from normally closed “loop” circuits. Keep all bystanders and rescue personnel out of the hazard area until OPPD representatives have declared the wires safe. Because we do not carry the necessary equipment to safely handle energized electrical wires, rescue attempts of victims in direct contact with a live source of electricity should be avoided until OPPD de-energizes the wires or until the proper rescue equipment is made available. ELECTRICAL EQUIPMENT FIRES Pole Mounted Transformers and Equipment Notify OPPD of the location and stand by until arrival of their representative. OPPD line crews may take up to an hour to respond, but a representative will be sent as soon as possible. If the fire is confined in metal-enclosed equipment such as a transformer, regulator, switch or pothead, it is usually the best practice to let it burn itself out. If the fire is burning the pole or cross arm so that equipment (including wires) may fall and be a hazard or cause damage, protect adjacent property and extinguish the fire by using a non-conducting extinguishing agent or a fog-type nozzle. If the pole has been burning for some time, or if there is any question about its integrity, evacuate a safe zone and let it burn. OPPD recommends – There is no reason for firefighters to be closer than 10’ from energized electrical equipment carrying 50,000 volts or less when using a fog stream. Energized electrical equipment carrying greater than 50,000 volts should not be approached.
Transformer Vaults – Manholes Transformer vaults are generally located in sidewalks, at the curb line or in alleys. When located in alleys, the door opening and vent grating are generally located on the north side of the alley. Vault doors are always locked, and an OPPD representative will have a key. See Figure 2. Manholes and transformer vaults fall under the definition of a confined space. A combustible gas indicator should be used to determine if explosive Figure 2: Electrical vault in sidewalk. gases are present, and if positive pressure ventilation should be applied. SCBA must always be worn because of the changing nature of confined spaces. Confined space procedures shall be followed when a fire in a manhole or vault is encountered. A Rescue company shall be requested, and shall be on location prior to firefighte r entry into any vault or manhole in order to perform confined space entry permitting procedures. Keep in mind that the fire or explosion usually has done its maximum damage when the failure first occurred, it is not imperative that firefighters take actio n until OPPD personnel check conditions. When necessary, a carbon dioxide or a dry chemical extinguisher can be directed into the vault to extinguish burning materials. If smoke is issuing from a manhole or vault, do not attempt to remove the cover. If the cover has been removed, do not attempt to replace it. When manhole covers need to be removed for operations, a monitor probe should be used to test the atmosphere in the space below before attempting removal. Dragging 100 lb. manhole lids off of their openings can create sparks that will ignite an explosive atmosphere. Keep the public at a safe distance from the manhole. If necessary, stop traffic at each end of the block. If fire is confined to a vault, STAY OUT until the OPPD representative advises that it is safe to enter. The possibility of toxic and/or explosive gases in enclosures containing electrical equipment is always present. Before ventilating a vault structure, consideration should be given to the current level of gases present. Adding fresh air during ventilation may bring the contaminated atmosphere into its flammable range and result in an explosion. Full protective equipment and SCBA must be worn when performing emergency operations. Some vaults are equipped
with ventilator shutters, which close when thermal links melt. Where such installations exist, permitting fresh air to enter too soon is undesirable. DISCONNECTING POWER TO A BUILDING The Fire Department should not deprive occupants of a building electrical service unless it is absolutely necessary. When the necessity does arise, it is desirable to shut off only that section affected. This may be a certain floor, department, machine or group of machines. If the Fire Department cuts off the service with a switch, however, the switch must be locked - out or somehow monitored to ensure that it is not turned on until it is certain that it can be turned on safely.

Omaha Fire Department personnel shall not remove electrical meters. Fire Department personnel should never cut wires because we do not have the proper equipment or training to cut them safely. Voltage Testers Philmore AC Voltage Testers have been purchased and are carried on all OFD fire apparatus. These testers can be used when there is a need to identify a specific electrical circuit. This is usually necessary when there has been a small fire at or near an electrical outlet. The tester can be used to isolate the circuit for de-energizing. By doing this, the rest of the circuits in a structure can remain energized. To maintain an adequate safety margin, the Philmore tester should only be used on 110-volt circuits, such as standard electrical outlets and light switch circuits. The outlet being tested should be secure from moving to prevent accidental contact with exposed wires. The Philmore tester shall not be used to test downed electrical lines or other loose wires. The Philmore tester should not be used in wet conditions, such as standing wate r, water soaked carpets or when gloves and other protective clothing is soaked with water. Use of the Philmore tester should also be avoided in low-light conditions, as there is more of a risk of coming into contact with an energized circuit. To utilize the tester, the two ends can be inserted into the receptacles of an outlet. If the circuit is energized, the tester will “light up” GENERAL SAFETY GUIDELINES Placing ladders or pike poles less than 10 feet from power lines should be avoided during any fire or rescue operation, including aerial ladders. Always assume that all wires and equipment are energized and that they may cause serious bodily harm. Persons of nervous temperament or with any form of heart trouble should never be exposed to even a slight shock. Fire Department personnel should attempt to avoid any contact with electrical equipment and/or wires. If you cannot identify the status of a piece of equipment or wire, consider it live until proven otherwise.
Caution must be used any time ladders are used near power lines, as contact with overhead wires may cause serious burns or electrocution. Placing ladders less than 10 feet from power lines should be avoided. We are always careful when working around electricity with metal ladders because we know the shock potential. That same measure of caution should also be exercised when working with fiberglass ladders. Even though fiberglass is a so - called “non-conductor”, if the surface of the ladder is dirty or damp, the potential exists for transmission of the current across the surface to the person holding the ladder. The same holds true for wooden and fiberglass pike poles. They cannot be considered safe for electrical contact unless they are clean, dry, and treated with a special silicone coating. Caution should also be used when apparatus, especially aerials, are working around energized wires. If a wire falls onto or comes in contact with the apparatus, extra cauti on must be taken when exiting to avoid electrical shock. The firefighter should jump clear of the apparatus instead of stepping off. Because of “step potential” an individual should “hop”, not walk, away from the apparatus. When it is necessary to operate an electrical switch while standing on a wet surface, in water, or when the switch is wet, the switch must not be grasped with the fingers or palm of the hand. Electricity has a tendency to cause the muscles to contract, which would prevent opening the hand to release the object. Use a non-conducting object, such as an axe handle, to operate the switch. Rubber fire boots will not provide protection from shock. When investigating gas odors or leaks, or when any other potentially explosive atmosphere is encountered, firefighters should not operate electrical switches. The arc caused by “making” or “breaking” the contact in a switch is hot enough to ignite gas vapors, thereby causing an explosion. It is always the best choice, when possible, to disconnect or shut off the power at a site remote from switches which are of concern. APPENDIX Definitions Shock – An extreme stimulation of the nerves by the passage of electric current through the body. An individual contacting electrical equipment, an electric wire which is live, or an object which is in contact with such wire may receive a fatal shock or a shock that may cause him/her to lose control of their actions and fall. For this reason, contact with any current should be avoided. The danger is greater when one is standing in water or on wet ground.
Fatal shocks have been received when firefighters and others have walked into areas with “step potential” or into puddles in which wires have fallen. Volt – The force that will cause a current of one ampere to flow through a conductor whose resistance is one ohm. Ampere – The standard unit for measuring the strength of an electric current; amount of current sent by one volt through a resistance of one ohm. Ohm – The unit of electrical resistance equal to the resistance of a circuit in which an electromotive force of one volt maintains a current of one amp. Step Potential – Step potential is caused by the flow of fault current through the earth. The current flow creates a voltage drop at the earth’s surface. A person standing with feet apart bridges a portion of this drop. This places a potential difference from foot to foot. As one walks closer to a downed wire, the “step potential” increases. When a person steps from an area of lower potential to an area of higher potential, the current passes through the body because this is the path of least resistance. The best way to avoid succumbing to step potential is to “hop” away from an energized wire. Touch Potential – Touch potential is a problem similar to step potential. It involves a fault current flow in the earth establishing a potential difference between the earth contact point and some remote hardware. Qualified Person - A person who, by possession of a recognized degree, certificate, professional standing, or skill, and who, by knowledge, training, and experience has demonstrated ability to deal with problems related to the subject matter, the work, or the project. HIGH-RISE ELEVATOR OPERATIONS AND PRECAUTIONS Elevator use during a high-rise fire incident is a calculated risk. As per the ICS 8-0 ICS for High-Rise Operations SOP, elevators will be coordinated and controlled by the position of Elevator Control under the supervision of Lobby Control. Elevator Control will assign elevator operators for the duration of the incident. The following are operational considerations and precautions regarding elevator use by fire department personnel during a high -rise fire incident.    Avoid elevator use unless it will greatly improve operations. Elevator use may be considered if the reported fire floor is above the 6 th floor. For access to areas at or below the 6th floor personnel should utilize the stairwell.    DO NOT use elevators that do not have firefighter service (Phase II control) or are not under the control of fire department personnel.    Always capture (Phase I) all elevators and recall to lobby at EVERY fire incident, including still alarms. This takes the elevators out of service so occupants cannot use them for evacuation purposes. Ensure that all elevators are accounted for and are checked for victims.
   If the decision is made to utilize elevators to move personnel quickly to the Staging area two floors below the fire floor, use the elevators early in the incident. Loss of elevators (due to water/smoke in the elevator shaft or elevator malfunction) generally occurs approximately 20 minutes into a high-rise operation.    Crews going aloft utilizing elevators should stop the elevator every five floors. This confirms that fire personnel are still in control of the elevator. If the elevator does not stop and is no longer under their control press the emergency stop. If the emergency stop does not work the doors should be forced open to stop the car.    During the stop at every five floors personnel should evaluate the shaft for water, smoke or fire if possible.    If the elevator has a roof hatch, open it to check the shaft for smoke/water.    Listen for water running down the shaft.    NEVER take the elevator to the fire floor or floor indicated by the alarm system. Always stop and get off at least two floors below. When the initial fire attack companies arrive two floors below the fire floor they should quickly familiarize themselves with the floor arrangement, as the layout of the fire floor will probably be similar if not the same.    Look at the hallway evacuation map and find the location of the stairwells, room and corridor locations, etc.    NEVER utilize an elevator to travel to a floor above the fire floor unless that particular elevator bank DOES NOT serve the fire floor (Blind shaft).    Before using in Phase II operation, check for the flashing fire helmet light near the “Firefighter emergency service” key switch. This flashing light indicates an alarm activation in the elevator machine room, lobby, or hoist way. In those cases, do not use that bank of elevators.    When the determination is made that using elevators to transport personnel is no longer safe, consideration may be made to use the elevators to transport equipment up to Staging. To do this, put the elevator back into regular mode so equipment can be loaded, the floor button for the staging area can be pressed, and the car can be sent up without anyone onboard. Ensure that occupants have been evacuated or controlled so that no civilians attempt to use the elevator that is now in regular mode and will respond to calls to individual floors.    Never overcrowd elevators or exceed the weight limit with personnel wearing PPE and equipment. Allow enough room for personnel to don SCBA in the elevator should the need arise.    Personnel using elevators, including designated elevator operators should wear PPE/SCBA, have a portable radio and a flashlight. Forcible entry tools should be carried by personnel or should be available in the car. Elevator operato rs should also have an extra SCBA cylinder in the car.
ELEVATOR RESCUE/PERSON TRAPPED Although the concept of elevators transporting people is relatively simple, extrication of trapped occupants can often be difficult. Consider the following possible conditions:    An elevator may have stopped between floors.    Loss of electrical power has resulted in a dark elevator with no ventilation.    Passengers may be hysterical, panicked, or in need of medical care. These conditions are further complicated by the fact that elevator technology constantly changes. There are numerous elevator manufacturers, each having produced a variety of elevators. It is not uncommon for a single elevator to have features and special equipment offered by several manufacturers, resulting in a wide variety of elevators with unique characteristics. TYPES OF ELEVATORS Currently, there are two types of elevators: 1. Cable operated elevators (sometimes referred to as an electric or traction elevator) 2. Hydraulic operated elevators Cable Elevators A typical cable elevator consists of an elevator shaft for the car to travel within, an elevator car, cables attached to the car, counterweights, vertical tracks, emergency safety brakes, and an equipment room for the electrical equipment (electric motor and winding drum) to raise or lower the elevator. Cable elevators are drum type or traction type. Drum Type A winding drum type elevator consists of a car attached to one or more cables that pass around a winding drum and to a moving counterweight. Counterweight cables are on one side of the drum and car cables are on the other side of the drum. The drum is grooved for cable movement. Both sets of cables run in the same grooves (counterweight cables unwind when car cables wind and vice-versa). The equipment room for drum type may be located in a basement or on the roof of a building. Limitations in the length and diameter of the winding drum restricts this type of elevator to
150-foot lifts and slow speeds. Equipment for this application requires more space than other types of elevators. For this reason, it is no longer manufactured. Traction Type Traction type elevators are used for higher lifts and greater speeds than drum type elevators and use a traction sheave instead of a winding drum. In traction type elevators, cables are attached to the car and pass over a traction sheave to counterweights. In this configuration, cables passing over the traction sheave unwind as fast as they wind and car speed is dependent on the size of the traction sheave and the electric motor speed. There are two types of traction equipment:    High speed direct traction or gear-less type traction consists of a slow speed DC motor directly coupled to a traction sheave with a brake wheel mounted on the motor shaft.    Geared traction type uses a high-speed motor. The motor is geared to a traction sheave through worm gears with a brake wheel between the worm gears and motor. Since there is less weight and equipment with traction type elevators, the equipment room is usually located on the roof or above an elevator shaft in high-rise buildings. Hydraulic Elevators Similar in general design to the cable elevator, hydraulic elevators use hydraulic power instead of cables and traction sheaves and are generally limited to six story buildings. In very old installations, a car is raised or lowered by a ram powered by water pressure. Newer installations use oil pressure to power a ram. Some less modern hydraulic elevators and hydraulic elevators that travel over six stories may use cables and counterweights. The equipment rooms for hydraulic elevators are normally located at the lower level of a building, but can be at any floor level or 50 to 100 feet away from the elevator shaft. The equipment room contains the following main components:    Controller or relay panel (electronics necessary to run the elevator).    Hydraulic power unit. The hydraulic power unit consists of a reservoir for the oil supply, hydraulic pump, and valves to raise or lower the elevator car.
PASSENGER ELEVATORS Passenger elevators (cable and hydraulic) are designed to quickly move passengers to different levels within a multi-story building. Modern passenger elevators are completely automated and are under the control of an electronic computer, which constantly evaluates the needs and demands of the system. Computers constantly make adjustment, move cars, and work to meet current demands. Cars are sent to the area of greatest need and when cars are not needed, they are allowed to rest or sleep. Passenger elevators are norma lly faster than freight elevators and often use express elevators that cannot stop at all floors. FREIGHT ELEVATORS Freight elevators (cable and hydraulic) are generally less complicated and serve a different purpose than passenger elevators. Larger than passenger elevators, freight elevators can be as large as 12 feet by 14 feet and have a carrying capacity of up to three tons. Generally, freight elevators are separate from the main lobby of a building and can have a street, alley, or loading dock access. Not being under computer control, freight elevators are simpler to operate and control than passenger elevators and normally service an entire building from the lowest to highest level. Freight elevators are normally slower than passenger elevators and can stop at all floors unless a special override control is activated. ELEVATOR DOORS Modern elevators utilize two sets of doors for operation and passenger ingress/egress. The door on the elevator car is referred to as the inner or car door and travels with the elevator car. The door that is seen from each floor of a building is referred to as the outer or hoist way door. This hoist way door is a part of the building (each landing). It is important to realize that the car door does all the work; the hoist way door is a dependent. These doors can be opened or closed by electric motors, or manually for emergency incidents. Safety devices are located at each landing to prevent inadvertent hoist way door openings and to prevent an elevator car from moving unless a door is in a locked position. All types of doors are designed with a safety feature that will cause the car to stop whenever a car door is opened. There are four basic types of doors used on elevators:    Swing hall doors consists of a hoist way door that is manually opened and a single slide car door that is power operated or a collapsible gate.
   Single slide doors are power operated single panel doors.    Center opening doors consist of two power-operated panels that part simultaneously with a brisk, noiseless motion.    Two speed doors consist of two power-operated panels that are geared together. One door moves twice as fast as the other door so that both doors will meet concurrently in the open position. A power unit that is located on top of the elevator car normally opens elevator doors. When an elevator car is level with a floor landing, the power unit moves the car door open or closed. A pick-up arm (clutch, vane, bayonet, or cam) contacts rollers on the hoist way door, which releases the door latch on the hoist way door. The power unit opens the car door, which in turn opens the hoist way door. The door rollers and pick-up arm may be different on various elevators but they all work on the same principle. BRAKES Elevators are provided with brakes for normal operation and are similar to automotive brakes. A centrifugal force governor is provided on most elevators to guard against over -speeding (when a car travels in excess of 20% of top speed, the governor will activate a safety stop device). Safeties are installed at the bottom of an elevator car and occasionally on counterweights to provide positive emergency stopping when activated by the governor. If necessary, abrupt stops are possible with this equipment. On elevators capable of high speeds, wedges are forced between two jaws and the vertical rail is gripped with increasing force to slow a runaway elevator car to a gradual stop. EXTRICATION OF TRAPPED PERSON NOTE: The following procedures shall only be utilized when an actual emergency exists; medical or otherwise. These procedures shall not be used indiscriminately. If time permits, and conditions warrant, crews will await the arrival of an elevator repairperson. (Between floors or at a landing?)    What is the type of elevator? (Hydraulic or Cable)    Where is the elevator equipment room? (Normally, above for a cable elevator and below for a hydraulic elevator) Once on scene there are general guidelines for rescue to be considered. Various operations can be utilized to remove trapped passengers from an inoperative elevator. Use of a particular operation should be based on the needs and conditions of each incident and the expertise of personnel. When it has been determined there is an inoperative elevator with trapped occupants, the following considerations should be initially implemented:    Position personnel equipped with portable radios at the following locations:  Elevator equipment room (access to main power switches and elevator equipment).  Floor where elevator is inoperable (elevator rescue procedures will be coordinated from this location).    Establish voice contact with the trapped elevator passengers and:  Determine if trapped passengers are in need of medical assistance, or if conditions are stable. Do not rescue passengers using forcible entry methods unless other methods are unsuccessful and they are in need of immediate medical attention. Forcible entry methods can be dangerous to passengers and fire service personnel, and will cause damage to an elevator.  If conditions are stable, inform the passengers they are safe and will be removed from the inoperative elevator in a short period of time. It is also advantageous to keep trapped passengers informed of operations that are being utilized to remove them.  Have the trapped passengers verify the status of the Emergency Stop Button. Remember that if an elevator is stalled due to a malfunction, (one that can be quickly corrected, i.e. overheated relay, loss of power from an activated main switch, etc.), it is necessary for the Emergency Stop Button to be activated before power will be returned to an inoperative elevator. (Placed in the normal/run position.)
   Instruct the passengers to push the Door Open Button (if so equipped).    If the elevator car is within a few inches of the landing floor, and the power is off in the equipment room, instruct the passengers to try to manually open the car door. This may require some effort as the car door operates the hoist way door through a clutch mechanism. Moving the car door will release the latch on the hoist way door and allow the door to be opened. Personnel in the equipment room should:    Check the electrical circuits (main switch, fuses, etc.) to verify if powe r is on or off to the elevator. Occasionally, circuits are tripped due to overheating and can be safely reset.    If there is power to the elevator, turn the power off for at least 30 seconds and then back on again. This can reactivate the elevator by allowing relays to reset (at least 30 seconds is necessary to clear any previous programming in the elevator computer). If the elevator car is within a few inches of the landing, the door may be opened.    If there is a Door Open Button in the elevator, instruct the passengers to again push this button and the doors may open.    If an elevator is equipped with a recall system, a key may be used to recall the elevator to the ground floor and open its doors. If the elevator returns to the ground floor but does not open its doors, instruct the passengers to push the Door Open Button (if so equipped). NOTE: Do not attempt to rescue trapped passengers from an inoperative elevator unless the power to the elevator has been disconnected. This requires that a person be assigned to the main power switch until the rescue is completed. OPENING OF THE HOIST WAY DOOR At times it may be necessary to open the hoist way door. HELICOPTER SAFETY GUIDELINES Helicopter safety involves being constantly alert and aware of the limitations involved with helicopter landing, loading, and take-offs. Remember, most ground-related helicopter accidents involve contact with the main or tail rotor blades. Noise produced by fully powered helicopters greatly reduces the ability to hear and further reduces the ability to hear warnings or instructions from flight crews.
The following safety procedures will be standard operating procedure for all incidents involving helicopter operations:    Approach to the helicopter will be from the front only. No firefighter will approach any helicopter without being directed by the flight crew of the aircraft involved. Again, approaches to helicopters by Fire Department personnel will be done only under the direction and guidance of helicopter flight personnel.    The tail rotor of a helicopter under power is the most dangerous part of a helicopter, and it is for this reason that all approaches to landed helicopters will be from the front. (Figure #2, Page 9)    Do not chase loose items dislodged by rotor downwash.    Wear your helmet and make certain that it is securely fastened to your head.    Use the helmet face shield to protect your eyes from flying debris. Do not look directly into rotor downwash.    Do not allow lights to be directed on the helicopter during landing or take-offs. Apparatus headlights should be on low beam. Do not use spot lights or white strobe lights. Be certain there are no photographer flood lights in the vicinity of the helicopter landing site.    Package patient properly and protect against flying debris. Strap down loose blankets and linen. Cover the patient's face to protect from flying debris.

ICE/WATER RESCUE TEAM EQUIPMENT Designated Ice/Water rescue teams are assigned to all Truck Companies. Each Ice Rescue Team has been issued the following equipment:    2 Mustang Survival suits, 2 pairs of ankle weights and 2 pairs of rescue fins    1 pair Ice Awls    2 ice rescue carabiners and 2 aluminum standard locking “D” carabiners    1 ice rescue board    2 - 200’ lengths lightweight floating rope with storage bags    1 large mesh storage bag    1 ice rescue sling    2 pair ice crampons    2 ice screws Mustang Survival Suits Ice Awls The ice awls are used for self-rescue and should be carried by looping the rope over the back of the rescuer’s neck to provide easy access if needed.
Ice Rescue Carabiners A Carabiner is a large metal hook that allows for quick, easy attachment to the tether line when wrapping the rope around a victim. The locking “D” carabiners are used for attaching the tether rope to the rescuer. Carabiners should be inspected twice a year (spring and fall) for bends and cracks and lubricated with a silicone based lubricant. Ice Rescue Board The light weight Ice Rescue Board combines the highly maneuverable water rescue board with a special reinforced nylon cover. The cover slips over the board and is secured with a heavy duty zipper, providing 4 handles and 2 metal attachment points that assist with victim retrieval. The Ice Rescue Board should be inspected twice a year (spring and fall) for rips, tears, and separations. Clean with mild soapy water if necessary and air dry (keep out of the sun). Lightweight Floating Rope Each Ice/Water Rescue Team is equipped with 2 - 200’ highly visible rope. It is stored in a nylon bag with draw strings on both ends which allow the rope to be deployed from either end. This rope should be inspected annually, cleaned with clear water and air dried. Note: This rope is for water/ice rescue work only!!! Mesh Equipment Bag A large mesh bag has been provided for the ice rescue teams to make the transport of equipment to the scene less cumbersome. All assigned equipment for ice rescue should be carried in this bag. SCENE SIZE UP Procedures to be employed in conducting an ice rescue differ depending on many factors. The Incident Commander must consider the following scene conditions when sizing up an incident. Environmental Conditions 1. Weather - Are the weather conditions such that exposure to victims and rescuers is extreme? 2. Ice Conditions - While knowing the different types of ice and the factors affecting it may help in understanding its potential strength, FOR RESCUE PURPOSES NO ICE SHOULD BE CONSIDERED SAFE. All rescuers must be wearing life jackets or cold water suits before leaving shore.
3. Access to the incident - The victim may be too far from shore to attempt rescue and access to the shore where the rescue will be based may be quite a distance from the road. Is there a way to improve the timeliness of the response team? Location and condition of the victim(s) 1. How many victims are there? 2. Are victims conscious, alert, and able to help themselves? This is the most important factor used in prioritizing rescues. 3. Are victims unresponsive or unconscious, or unable to help themselves? If so, these victims are priority victims. 4. Is there a mechanism of injury that indicates the possibility of injury? 5. If the victim has become submersed, what was the last seen point? Use landmarks to provide the dive team a precise location when they arrive. Contact with bystanders / witnesses 1. TIME - How long the victim has been exposed or submersed is critical information. If the victim is recovered in less than one hour after submersion has occurred, maximum efforts at resuscitation should be attempted. 2. Have other victims already become submersed? At what time? 3. Could the victim be injured as well as hypothermic? RESCUE The Omaha Fire Department uses a two-tiered approach when responding to ice rescue incidents. If a rescue attempt is to be initiated, the Incident Commander will develop an operational plan based on scene size-up, choosing the victim priority and the best way to approach the victim. Rescuer safety is of primary concern. Constant communication with the victim must be maintained throughout the rescue effort. The first responding company should attempt to reach the victim with a pike pole. When the victim is beyond reaching distance, the firefighters should attempt to reach him/her with the throw rope and pull the victim to safety. When the victim is beyond the reach of the rope, throw them the ice awls and/or tell them how to do self-rescue. Remember, the BEST RESCUE is a DRY RESCUE!!! When an Ice/Water Rescue Team arrives, all efforts of the first arriving companies should immediately be redirected by the Incident Commander toward supporting Ice/Water Rescue Team response. Support would include carrying the response team’s equipment so the rescuers can conserve their energy for the rescue effort. . ICE RESCUE TEAM RESPONSE An Ice/Water Rescue Team response is a rescue effort by designated Ice/Water Rescue Teams that includes the use of cold water suits and other rescue equipment assigned to these companies. The following guidelines are meant to provide a tactical approach that is effective,
but they must be adapted to fit the situation. Deviation from these guidelines may be appropriate when necessary. Arrival at the Scene Immediately upon arrival of an ice rescue team, the officer on the team will report to the Incident Commander and decide on a rescue technique and victim priority. He/she should also ensure that communications with the victim is continuous throughout the rescue effort. Two members of the team will don the Mustang suits, one who will attempt the rescue and the other who will be a back-up rescuer should the first rescuer be unable to accomplish the rescue. This back-up rescuer shall be committed to the priority victim until the victim is secured. NOTE: Team members shall not don the suits while the apparatus is responding to the scene. Another member of the team will be the line tender. This member’s duties include setting up the pre-rigged equipment, assisting the rescuers with the suits, hooking up their safety lines, and monitoring and supervising the tether lines during rescue and retrieval of the victim. Rescue The D-rings on the front of the primary rescuer’s Mustang suit are attached to the end of a rope with a locking carabiner. A rescue carabiner is connected to the rope with a Figure 8 on a bight 3 to 4 feet from rescuer. This line attachment serves two purposes; it serves as a tether that allows the shore team to pull the rescuer and the victim back to shore, and it is used to secure the victim. All rescuers should have ice awls with them when on the ice. Approach to the victim should be at a 45 degree angle from shore and the rescuer should approach from behind or beside the victim to avoid breaking the ice shelf that the victim is holding onto. If the rescue carabiner is in the rescuer’s right hand, he/she should approach the victim from the right to keep from tangling the line when wrapping it around the victim. The rescue carabiner should be carried in the open position from the time the rescuer leaves the shore. As the rescuer approaches the victim, he/she should begin to crawl. This will distribute the rescuer’s weight and allow him/her to get closer to the victim without breaking the ice. As the rescuer gets closer to the edge, he/she should lay down and roll keeping the tethered line above his/her head to avoid tangles. The rescuer should enter the ice hole from behind or beside the victim. Caution -Avoid entering the water too quickly or falling through the ice while wearing the cold water suit as the increased air pressure in the suit may cause injury to the ears. Approach the victim carefully while maintaining verbal contact. Form a loop around the victim from behind with the line from shore and the ice rescue carabiner. This loop should be placed
as high as possible under the victim’s arms, and the rescuer should hold the victim from behind until the line is pulled tight. DO NOT LET GO OF THE VICTIM ONCE PHYSICAL CONTACT HAS BEEN ESTABLISHED. When the loop is secured, the rescuer gives the line tender the “retrieve” signal by touching the top of his/her head with the palm of his/her hand. The shore team then pulls the slack out of the line which further secures the victim and begins to pull him onto the ice. The shore team should pull the rope at a slow, steady pace to reduce the possibility of injuries to the victim and the rescuer. The rescuer should assist by gently lifting the victim out of the water. The rescuer will then be pulled from the water using the same line. Using the Ice Rescue Board Using the Ice Rescue Board is the method of choice for rope rescue responders. The Ice Rescue Board can be used to:    Serve as floatation across open water    Extend as a reach tool    Serve as a buffer for the victim, allowing for gentle handling The Ice Rescue Board is pre-rigged by passing a rope through the rings at the top and middle of the board, with the front of the board facing the shore. This allows the board to move freely on the tether line. After the rope is passed through the rings, a Figure 8 on a bight is tied in the end of the rope, and a locking D carabiner is attached to the end of the rope and to the rescuer. The rescue carabiner is then attached to the rope about 18 to 24 inches from the rescuer using a directional Figure 8. A rescue using the ice rescue board can be made by running extra line through the rings on the board, doing the rescue as outlined above, and sliding the victim onto the board either after they are out of the water or while they are still in the water. Proper victim placement on the board is achieved as the rescue carabiner will stop at the middle board loop, placing the victim’s chest midway on the board. The victim can then be pulled to shore. Initial Dispatch A Still Alarm response will be dispatched along with a Medic Unit and Rescue 33. For example, a response to Carter Lake might include E21, T21, M21, B2, and Rescue 33. FC71 & TC1 will be notified of possible ice water rescue. If known victim in the water FC71 & TC1 will be notified to respond. All other resources are requested at the discretion of on scene commander. Dive Team Victim Recovery The initial response will include R33, Medic Unit, FC71 and TC1. FC2 will be notified of the incident prior to dispatch of units for approval. The Incident Commander should bring the members of the ice rescue team and the dive rescue team together to coordinate the recovery effort. The dive team will then immediately begin their recovery efforts. Once the diver
recovers the victim and surfaces, an ice rescue team member, with suit donned, can enter the water and assist in getting the body out of the water. Only those required for the recovery should be on the ice, and they should be reminded not to stand close together. When the victim is recovered after submersion has occurred, maximum efforts at resuscitation should be attempted following current EMS protocols. Safety Precautions It is imperative that during any ice rescue operation, safety for emergency responders must be the highest priority. The following safety precautions shall be applied to all ice rescue operations. A. All personnel operating at any water or ice rescue incident must wear a life jacket or the cold water suit. B. All rescuers shall be tethered. If victims are beyond reach of a tether, no rescue will be attempted. All ice rescue team members shall be tethered to shore or to an ice screw. C. Use caution to enter the water slowly when wearing a cold water suit. Too fast an entry could increase the air pressure in the suit enough to cause injury to the ears. D. The United States Coast Guard recommends that the youngest, most physically fit, trained members of the responding companies be used as rescuers. Ice rescue operations will stress the cardiovascular system of the rescuer. E. Swift water (river current) rescue should ONLY be performed by those individuals that are trained for swift water rescue. F. The Incident Commander should consider calling the Salvation Army during initial response. The Salvation Army will provide heated shelter for clothing changes along with restroom facilities should the incident become prolonged.

OPERATIONS 25-0 RAPID INTERVENTION CREW REVISION HISTORY REVISION #    REVISION DESCRIPTION      DATE REVISED      AUTHOR         REVIEWED BY      APPROVED BY 1            Periodic Revision        OCT 2015       Stolinski 787    McEvoy 680         Hunter 723 Terminology & Radio 2                                     Dec 2017       Langlois 888      Ryan 679        McCormick 638 updates PURPOSE To establish policy and procedure for the initial actions of the Initial Rapid Intervention Crew (IRIC) and, as more resources arrive, the Rapid Intervention Crew (RIC) in order to proactively prepare the fire ground for firefighter survival, and to take a ppropriate actions in response to missing or trapped firefighters. The department addresses each type of technical specialty, and their associated backup team structure, in their respective SOPs. This SOP deals strictly with structural fire incidents, and incidents of a similar nature. This SOP does not address hazmat, confined space, trench rescue, dive rescue, or other types of technical rescue incidents. DEFINITIONS Initial Rapid Intervention Crew (IRIC): At least two members of a first-due company, who shall remain outside and available for rescue of firefighters entering an IDLH situation, should the need arise, (i.e. the FAE, and FF#2). Rapid Intervention Crew (RIC): A four-person company dedicated to firefighter safety and survival, who shall perform actions to prepare the structure for firefighter emergency egress, and who shall remain prepared for rapid firefighter location and rescue, should the need arise. This crew takes the place of the IRIC as soon as possible after arriving on the fire scene. Hazardous/Immediately Dangerous to Life and Health (IDLH) atmosphere: Any atmosphere that is oxygen deficient or that contains a toxic or disease producing contaminant. This condition would do one or more of the following:     Pose an immediate or delayed threat to life     Cause irreversible adverse health effects     Interfere with an individual’s ability to escape unaided from a hazardous environment. Any structure fire beyond the incipient stage is considered to have an IDLH atmosphere.

--- OPS 25-0 ---
Dynamic Risk Assessment (DRA): Recognition of transitory hazards that are sure to exist at some point during the incident, evaluation of those hazards, and application of a though process that justifies choice of procedures/tactics. The building condition, the fire progress, and the operating situation will be evaluated over and over again, and appropriate actions shall be taken to preserve the life and safety of our firefighters. Hostile Fire Event (HFE): Backdraft / smoke explosion, flashover, or other uncontrolled rapid fire growth event. Anticipation of these events and pro-active prevention is critical. If they cannot be prevented, remove personnel immediately. OVERALL RIC STRATEGY It shall be the strategy of the Omaha Fire Department to put in place at each structural fire, or other similar IDLH incident: 1. A pro-active system to prepare the incident for firefighter survival, and to 2. Have in-place the tools and resources to assist and/or rescue firefighters that become lost or trapped. This strategy as applied to a structural fire will include the application of the following standards and regulations: OSHA 1910.134 Regulation on Respiratory Protection: (2 in/2 out - The Final Ruling) Requires no less than two personnel outside the IDLH atmosphere available for firefighter rescue if the need arises. One of the two individuals located outside the IDLH atmosphere may be assigned to an additional role, such as incident commander in charge of the emergency or safety officer, so long as this individual is able to perform assistance or rescue activities without jeopardizing the safety or health of any firefighter working at the incident. Nothing in this section is meant to preclude firefighters from performing emergency rescue activities before an entire team has assembled. NFPA 1500 Standard on Fire Department Occupational Safety and Health Program : Once additional personnel arrive, a designated Rapid Intervention Team of four (4) shall be established. These standards and regulations shall further be enhanced by the Omaha Fire Department on large or multiple-alarm incidents to require the deployment of multiple RICs to cover each point of offensive operation, and to form a coordinated overall strategy for such an incident. This may involve multiple Rescue companies, or additional Truck or Engine companies as other SOPs address, or Command sees fit.
IRIC/RIC Pro-Active Preparation This strategy shall initially incorporate tactics to prepare the structure for firefighter survival and self-rescue. This is accomplished by:    Completing a thorough, and continuing size-up of the structure and the fire    Communicating the RIC action plan,    Keeping complete accountability,    Preparing the proper equipment cache,    Preparing easy-to-locate egress points on each level where firefighters are operating (Softening the structure) This will require the first “2-out” firefighters (IRIC), and the rest of the RIC members as they arrive to actively “ENGAGE” the structure from the beginning, and throughout the incident. Using a DRA, the building condition, the fire progress, and the operating situation will be evaluated over and over again, and appropriate actions shall be taken to preserve the life and safety of our firefighters. The Officer of a RIC company shall avoid his/her company becoming part of the fire attack, extinguishment, and overhaul resources on scene of any fire, however, the IC will dictate. If assigned by a superior officer/IC to other than RIC duties, the RIC captain should remind the superior officer/IC of his/her company’s RIC designation, however, the RIC shall ultimately follow the decision/directives/orders of the officer/IC. In any case, the RIC should expect, to be a part of any rescue taking place, civilian or firefighter. If the IC deploys the RIC, whether to respond to a firefighter emergency or to any other duty, an additional RIC should be formed or special called. Rapid Intervention Crew Tactics IRIC (2-Out) The first-due engine company will comply with the 2-in / 2-out rule. The “2-out” will form the IRIC. Prior to entry, size up and a DRA will be performed to establish type of structure, availability of water supply, roof assembly involvement, location and extent of fire, pressurized smoke or the observance of impending hostile fire event, access and egress points, possibility of rescue and rescue profile, and ultimately offensive vs. defensive. Life-Saving Emergency Rescue Exception: The only exception to the 2-out rule is when the first arriving company determines that there is a confirmed viable re scue required, and that their immediate entry into the hazardous/IDLH atmosphere is necessary to save a life or prevent serious injury. IRIC Tactics include, but are not limited to:    Size-up (including Hot Lap/360 evaluation of incident, if possible) including: o Dynamic Risk Assessment (DRA)
o    Building Construction Type / Occupancy o    Rescue Profile o    Roof Assembly Involvement o    Fire/Smoke conditions and location  Pressurized Smoke or imminent Hostile Fire Event o Access / Egress Points o Water Supply o Observable Hazards o Adequate Attack Hose lines (length and size)    Communicate Size-up    Communicate RIC Action Plan    Communicate Needs    Initiate Action Plan / Document on Command Worksheet    Begin to soften structure and mark egress points as attack proceeds.    Ladder Egress Points above ground level.    Communicate location of egress points and hazards. RIC (Third-due Engine) When the third engine (or other company as designated by the IC) arrives on the scene, prior to Rescue 33 (R33) or Rescue 60 (R60), they will become the RIC, and will maintain their four-person crew for the same. This RIC will meet with the current IC and/or IRIC to receive any information available. They will continue to evaluate and soften the structure in order to enable firefighters to find their own way out easily and, secondly to enable a swift rescue of any lost or trapped firefighter. RIC shall at this point take over control and monitoring of the SEMS. The FAE of the third- due engine shall assume “Accountability” (See SWD 1-1 Personnel Accountability Procedures) and maintain this position at all times, until relieved by command of the IC. This position shall assume the radio designation “Accountability” and shall perform the following tasks:    Monitor SEMS for PASS activations    Monitor the designated TAC channel and A16 for MAYDAY transmissions.    Assist the IC with accountability of resources and ICS charting (See ICS 3-2 IFAW/TWS Worksheets)    Organize and maintain Personnel Accountability tags (PATs) during Level II Accountability procedures (See SWD 1-1 Personnel Accountability Procedures). At the discretion of the IC, Accountability may be assigned to any other unit, such as the Medic Unit. However in those instances when the Medic Unit is assigned Accountability, the IC should consider requesting a second medic be dispatched to standby for medical needs,
in order to avoid Accountability duties being abandoned in the event there is a civilian or firefighter patient or a MAYDAY where the medic unit will be pulled away for patient care. Monitor strategic mode (Offensive/Defensive)    Pull backup lines as necessary for personnel safety    Support RIC operations currently in place    Continue to soften structure    Communicate newly established egress points    Force entry but control egress points in coordination with ventilation plan RIC (Rescue Company or Replacement Company) R33 or R60 shall be dispatched to all fire alarms, or in their absence, a truck company shall be dispatched as a fill-in. RIC COMMUNICATIONS PLAN FOR MAYDAY DEPLOYMENT RIC TAC Channel: RIC will operate on the Mayday TAC channel that the fire fighter in distress is located on or possibly on A16 with an OEB activation and IC direction. All other personnel may be directed to move to the odd numbered TAC channel if ordered by the IC. Two situations are likely to happen when a firefighter calls, or attempts to call, a MAYDAY:    The FF in trouble will be able to and/or will hit the Orange Emergency Button (OEB) and go to A16    The FF in trouble will not be able to and/or will not hit the OEB and will stay on the TAC channel (ex: A4)
If the MAYDAY situation is not resolved/cleared in the first few minutes following the MAYDAY call, the IC, RIC, and Rescue Group Supervisor will have to quickly establish a Communications plan to address the MAYDAY situation. Establishing separate channels for each functional group as well as the distressed firefighter is one way to do this. It is important to segregate other fireground communications from that of the distressed firefighter whenever possible. This could be done either by keeping the distressed firefighter on the TAC channel and having everyone else switch to the odd numbered TAC channel or by the distressed firefighter activating their OEB and going to A16. Ultimately, the IC will decide. It is important to note that not every MAYDAY situation can be addressed in this S OP, and personnel may have to adjust their tactics and strategies, including the communications plan, to meet the needs of the current set of circumstances The RIC should communicate regularly with the Rescue Group Supervisor and/or IC, including an initial deployment acknowledgment as well as providing CAN status reports throughout the operation. (See OPS 10-0 Communications). In the event multiple RICs are established in different locations at the incident, they will be designated by their location. Examples: A RIC on side A (Alpha) will be “RIC Alpha Division”. A RIC on the 5th floor will be “RIC Division 5”.

RIC DEPLOYMENT OPERATIONS Criteria for deployment of the on-scene RIC includes, but is not limited to:    Any MAYDAY declared by anyone    Unaccounted for personnel resulting from a PAR    Sounds of a PASS device or SCBA low air alarm from inside the IDLH or activation of a PASS alarm at the GEMS monitor without communications and/or confirmation of the wellbeing of that firefighter.    Loss of communications with any crew/individual    Any time the Incident Commander deems it necessary.
Upon deployment for a firefighter rescue, the RIC should keep in mind that, depending on the building, fire conditions, circumstances of the rescue, etc., completion of the firefighter rescue may take successive RICs, each working in turn to extricate the firefighter from the building. RIC Captains should monitor their crews for such things as adequate air supply and physical exhaustion. RICs should ensure that they do not overextend themselves. Additional RICs or other resources should be requested early. Members of a RIC participating in the rescue of a down firefighter will be involved in a labor intensive and extremely emotional event. Do as much as you can, stay together, monitor your air and physical condition closely, call for another RIC to take over and continue the rescue, and exit the building together. OPS 28-0 WILDLAND/URBAN INTERFACE (WUI) RESPONSE REVISION HISTORY REVISION #        REVISION DESCRIPTION       DATE REVISED      AUTHOR       REVIEWED BY     APPROVED BY Original             Creation of SOP          May 2023       Werner 933      Salcedo 816    Bossman 729 Update #1                 Update               Sept. 2023     Werner933       Salcedo 816    Bossman 729 Purpose The purpose of this SOP is to establish a response procedure for the wildland/urban interface. This type of fire response will become more frequent as urban areas expand into the rural/wildland area. These undefined boundaries, topography, lack of water resources, and different tactics provide an unconventional challenge to the Omaha Fire Department. This SOP will outline the response of the Omaha Fire Department to these challenges. References    Jones and Bartlett, Fundamentals of Fire Fighter Skills, Chapter 21    Operations 5-1-5.1, Water Supply Task Force The term wildland-urban interface is used to explain the mixing of wildland with developed areas. The wildland-urban interface is defined as an area where undeveloped land with vegetative fuels is mixed with human-made structures. Because many people live in the wildland-urban interface, fires in this zone present a significant life safety hazard and a huge potential for property loss. Many parts of the wildland-urban interface do not have adequate municipal water systems. Fuels The primary fuel for wildland and ground fires is the vegetation in the area. Amounts of fuel in an area ranges from sparse grass to heavy underbrush and large trees. Fuels are classified as fine fuels or heavy fuels. Vegetative fuels can be located under the ground (roots), on the surface (grass and fallen leaves), or above the ground (tree branches). Fine fuels includes dried vegetation, such as twigs, leaves, needles, grass, moss, and light brush. Ground duff, the partly decomposed organic material on a forest floor, is another type of fine fuel. Fine fuels have a large surface area relative to their volume, which causes them to ignite easily and burn quickly and are usually the main type of fuel present in ground cover fires. Be aware that fires spread more quickly in fine fuels than in heavy timber and brush.
