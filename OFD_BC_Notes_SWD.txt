Gloves SELF CONTAINED BREATHING APPARATUS All Omaha Fire Department personnel operating in hazardous or IDLH environments must be equipped with and trained in the proper use and maintenance of Self-Contained Breathing Apparatus (SCBA). A proper face-to-skin seal is required; facial hair that interferes with the seal is not permitted. Members are responsible for their own compliance. Operations Division personnel must inspect their SCBA at the start of each shift, after use, and whenever needed to ensure readiness. Inspections must be documented per GO SWD 2021-01 Electronic Checklists. Any malfunctioning SCBA must be immediately removed from service, tagged, reported, and replaced through Light Air 30 or the appropriate Battalion Chief. Refer to OPS 6-0 for detailed procedures. A Hazardous/IDLH (Immediately Dangerous to Life and Health) atmosphere is any environment that is oxygen-deficient or contains toxic or infectious contaminants that may: •   Pose an immediate or delayed threat to life •   Cause irreversible adverse health effects •   Impair an individual's ability to escape without assistance All personnel operating in a Hazardous/IDLH atmosphere are required to use a Self- Contained Breathing Apparatus (SCBA). If there is any doubt about respiratory safety, at any incident, SCBA use shall be maintained. Detailed SCBA instructions can be found in the SCBA user manual SCBA Operating & Maintenance Instructions. Regulators must be disinfected at the start of each shift. Follow the procedures outlined in BUL TSD 2020-03 SCBA Disinfectant to ensure proper sanitation and compliance with department standards.
FULL PROTECTIVE CLOTHING While Responding All firefighting personnel must wear full protective clothing when responding to fire alarms, still alarms, or any situation where the protective qualities of the gear are needed. This requirement is optional for drivers, chief officers, and rescue squad personnel. Company officers may use their discretion in special situations (e.g., extended responses), but the intent of this policy is to ensure responders arrive on scene ready to operate safely and effectively. Drivers of engines, trucks, and special service units may forgo coats, hoods, gloves, and boots while en route, but must be prepared to don full protective gear quickly if the situation requires. Hearing Protection All Fire Department personnel are required to wear hearing protection when riding on or working near fire apparatus running at high idle or above, or when operating high-noise power tools. Seat Belts All occupants must wear seat belts in any moving Fire Department vehicle or apparatus. Personnel must be properly dressed, seated, and belted before the vehicle is in motion. Seat belts must remain fastened while the vehicle is moving; donning protective gear while en route is prohibited. Tampering with seat belt alarms or bypassing safety mechanisms will result in disciplinary action. While on the Fire Ground Full protective clothing must be worn at all times on the fire ground. •   Hoods must be worn up during firefighting. •   Gloves are required during any task with potential hand injury. •   Helmet face shields must be used when eye protection is needed and SCBA facepiece is not worn. •   High-visibility vests must be worn on roadways, except during active fire suppression, extrication, or IDLH conditions. Vests must be put back on once the hazard is mitigated. NOTE: In specific situations for which no guidelines have been issued, the proper protective clothing to protect against all foreseeable hazards shall be worn.
Safety Eyewear Operators of power saws or hydraulic rescue tools must wear department-issued safety eyewear when SCBA is not required. Helmet face shields are not sufficient for tool operators but must be used by nearby personnel. While at EMS Incidents All personnel must wear appropriate protective clothing during EMS incidents. Full gear is required when using forcible entry tools. Helmets and safety vests are mandatory near moving vehicles. The incident commander may allow exceptions when PPE interferes with tasks, such as patient care in confined spaces. Medic Personnel Medic personnel without full PPE must stage in a safe area designated by the IC. Only fully protected paramedics may enter the hazard zone to treat trapped patients, unless the IC approves an exception. Blue EMS gear is for bloodborne pathogen protection only and is not permitted in or near IDLH hot zones. Safety glasses should be worn to protect against bodily fluids. During Flood or Water Rescue Responses Personnel shall wear appropriate PPE for the environment, including PFD’s (Personal Flotation Devices), rescue helmets, and high-visibility vests. PURPOSE To reduce the risks of exposure to harmful contaminants to OFD personnel during fire and post- fire operations. POST-FIRE RESPIRATORY PROTECTION Although it is possible to monitor for hazardous atmospheric conditions, such as CO, low O2 and HCN levels, this monitoring alone will not indicate that the atmosphere is safe to breathe without respiratory protection, due to the presence of other gases, toxins, carcinogens, etc. that may still be present in the atmosphere. These include, but are not limited to, benzene, formaldehyde, and polynuclear aromatic hydrocarbons. Even after ventilation, moving fire debris while performing overhaul and salvage can cause particulates to be introduced into the localized air, and gas pockets can be released, thus creating a health hazard for personnel. As a precaution and at the discretion of the IC and/or ISO air monitoring may still take place while any interior post-fire operations take place. The designated IC/Incident Safety Officer (ISO) shall ensure that these procedures continue throughout post-fire operations. 1. All personnel shall wear and use (breathing air) SCBA while operating in the post-fire atmosphere (i.e. overhaul, salvage). 2. Due to the additional physical stress on personnel operating with SCBA, the following procedures should be initiated: a. Captains/crew leaders should closely monitor their personnel for the beginning signs of overexertion and must be pro-active to prevent personnel from reaching the point of overexertion. b. Crews performing functions like overhaul must be rotated out to rehab more often, and replaced with fresh or rested crews.

ON SCENE DECONTAMINATION 1. Gross Decon: All personnel who entered the IDLH atmosphere, and any personnel’s PPE that has been contaminated with products of combustion, shall have their PPE and SCBA undergo gross decon, spraying down the gear and brushing with soapy water prior to removal of PPE. 2. Decon Wipes: After removing PPE, the decon wipes provided to each crew shall be used to wipe down the head and neck area, where the majority of the IDLH contaminants might have come in contact with skin through the hood. These wipes shall be used on scene and disposed of in the trash at the station. AT QUARTERS DECONTAMINATION 1. Shower and uniform change: The goal is to “Shower in an Hour”. The crew, if exposed to an IDLH atmosphere and products of combustion during a working fire incident, will return to quarters, and all personnel exposed to contaminants shall immediately shower and change into a clean uniform to remove contaminants on the skin. This shall be completed within an hour. If the crew is interrupted by calls during this hour, and it will cause them to go longer than an hour, the Captain has the option to take the crew out of service so showers can be completed within an hour.
2. Hood Exchange: All personnel have been issued a second hood. In order to reduce the contact time between the products of combustion and the individual’s skin, all personnel shall complete the following actions after every IDLH encountered: a. Remove the hood worn in the IDLH environment and place into the white mesh bag provided for each apparatus. b. Place in service your second hood, which will be kept on the apparatus each day. c. Each mesh bag may contain up to four hoods, and washed as a unit within the bag itself. d. Cleaning and drying of the hoods shall be in compliance with the procedures for cleaning structural firefighting PPE found in SOP SWD 1-0 Protective Equipment/Clothing. This cleaning shall occur at the earliest possible opportunity post IDLH exposure. 3. The cab of the apparatus will also be cleaned to remove as much contaminants as possible. 4. The SCBA shall be cleaned and decontaminated as per OPS 6-0 Self Contained Breathing Apparatus. Documentation of Exposures If it is believed that a significant exposure to an IDLH atmosphere (i.e., firefighter ran out of air or SCBA mask was displaced while in the IDLH), it shall be documented in the OFD Wellness reporting website on the OFD 6C Haz-Mat Exposure form.

However, we must also track what they are doing and where they are doing it. There are four keys to this functional accountability. DEFINITIONS Significant Exposure: A situation in which the body fluids, including blood, saliva, urine, respiratory secretions, or feces, of a source patient/individual have entered the body of an ESP (Emergency Services Provider) through a body opening including the mouth or nose, a mucous membrane, or a break in skin from cuts or abrasions, from a contaminated needlestick or scalpel, from intimate respiratory contact, or through any other situation when the patient’s body fluids may have entered the ESP’s body or when an airborne pathogen may have been transmitted from the patient or individual to the ESP. Contamination: Soiling or pollution, as by the introduction of blood or body fluids onto equipment, clothing, PPE, intact skin or turnout gear. EXPOSURE REPORTING PROCEDURES If an employee believes he/she has incurred a significant exposure while on duty, THE EMPLOYEE IS RESPONSIBLE for following the exposure reporting policy and procedures. Upon any exposure, immediately take care of the port of entry. Flush wounds, eyes or mucus membranes, remove contaminated clothing that may re-expose the site and if possible, disinfect the site. Mouthwash is available on the medic units for mucus membrane exposures of the mouth. Once decontaminated the exposed employee shall use the purple “Significant Exposure Report Folder” carried on all apparatus as a guide to properly report and treat the exposure. All significant exposures, incident or non-incident-related, will follow the “Reporting a Significant Exposure – KNOWN Source” or the “Reporting a Significant Exposure – UNKNOWN Source” as a guide. Significant exposures will also be documented as an “Injury on Duty” (see ADM 3-2 Injury/Illness Reporting Procedures) within the OFD Health and Wellness Website. NOTE: Bites (human/animal) that break the skin may be considered true exposures. Contact CorVel immediately for further assessment and possible treatment.

Nebraska statute (Neb. Rev. Stat. § 71 - 507 through § 71 - 513) provides protection against infectious diseases for persons that act as “Emergency Services Providers” (ESP) while delivering emergency medical care. An ESP in Nebraska is a certified out-of-hospital emergency care provider certified pursuant to the EMS act, sheriff, deputy sheriff, police officer, a state highway patrol officer, a funeral director, a paid or volunteer firefighter, and a person rendering emergency care gratuitously/Good Samaritan. (Neb. Rev. Stat. § 71 – 507(4)). You may have had a “significant exposure” (Neb. Rev. Stat. § 71 – 507 through 71-513) to an infectious disease if you provided emergency medical care to a patient/source person who was sick, injured, wounded, deceased or otherwise helpless or incapacitated and you believe that his/her blood or other body fluids, including airborne pathogens, may have entered your body. The infectious diseases described in Nebraska law and the rules and regulations of the Department of Regulation and Licensure - Nebraska Health and Human Services System include Hepatitis B, Hepatitis C, Meningococcal Meningitis, Active Pulmonary Tuberculosis, Human Immunodeficiency Virus, Diphtheria, Plague, Hemorrhagic Fevers, Rabies and such other diseases as the department may by rule and regulation specify. POST-EXPOSURE PROCEDURES Immediate action at the scene: Wash skin affected immediately with germicidal soap or soap and water. If mucous membranes are exposed, flush with water immediately. Remove contaminated clothing and package and tag as “biohazard” to avoid additional exposures. REMEMBER you are capable of infecting others after a significant exposure and should take the steps necessary to protect you and your family. Your designated physician should help you with any changes in lifestyle that may be necessary, even temporarily. PERTINENT SOURCE PERSON INFORMATION – Be sure to obtain as much information as you can at the scene or hospital. Both you and the person you assisted have the right to confidentiality. Once the source person leaves the scene you will not be able to obtain any patient information without the written permission of the patient, or if the patient is deceased or otherwise incapacitated without the written permission of his/her next of kin, legal guardian or personal representative. person is available for testing. It is difficult to get source persons tested after they are released from a facility. If the source person dies, it is impossible to test for infectious diseases after embalming. One of the contributing factors of stress is heat exposure. Heat exposure can result from extended operations in hot and humid weather, extended interior fire suppression activities and Haz-Mat operations requiring full encapsulation. The equipment we wear to protect ourselves from the heat also prevents the release of heat, which can contribute to the heat stress. One of the ways to reduce the potential for heat related injury is thorough proper rehabilitation during and after an extended incident. This SOP provides guidelines for initiating and operating a Rehab Group. Also included with this SOP are recommendations for post-incident rehabilitation. ESTABLISHING A REHAB GROUP Rehab shall be established at all working incidents, and any other time (training events, etc) deemed necessary by the IC, ISO or officers in charge of the event. Crews that were first to arrive at the incident should be sent to rehab and eventually put back into service first. When necessary, the IC should special call additional resources to assist with fire operations, post-fire ops and rotating crews through rehab.Working Fires, Training Exercises and Other “Routine” Incidents Rehab guidelines are based on the “Heat Stress Index” and the “Wind Chill Index” charts, which will be carried by the Safety Officer, Paramedic Shift Supervisor and on all Medic units. The heat stress index (HSI, see Table 1) is broken down into two categories: Gray – Danger, HSI above 105º F White – Caution, HSI at or below 105º F The wind chill index (WCI, see Table 2) is divided into three categories: Dark Gray – Great danger, WCI below -75ºF Light Gray – Increasing danger, WCI -25ºF to -75ºF White – Little danger, WCI above -25ºF

The area should be upwind and upgrade from the incident unless conditions dictate otherwise. Dispatch shall be notified so they can provide the location to incoming support personnel and apparatus. The Salvation Army Emergency Services Unit and other support vehicles, if at all possible, shall be located in the rehab area. The rehab area boundaries will be defined with fire-line tape whenever possible. This will help to control entry and exit of personnel. It will also prevent access of unauthorized personnel. It is desirable to have an area where personnel can go to get relief from the hot humid weather in the summer and cold and windy weather in the winter. The Salvation Army unit can be used as an area to cool down or warm up. In extreme weather conditions Incident Commanders should also consider utilizing a nearby business, if practical, or request a MAT bus for an on-site shelter
Staffing A minimum of two; preferably four, medic personnel are needed to staff a Rehab Group. The Incident Safety Officer shall ensure that rehab is being addressed. The EMS Battalion Chief, when available, shall be in charge of the Rehab Group. If the EMS Battalion Chief is not available, the Paramedic Shift Supervisor or a Lead Medic will assume command of the Rehab Group. CRITERIA FOR SENDING COMPANIES TO REHAB Summer Weather Structural Firefighting - Caution rule on heat stress index    Personnel involved in interior firefighting operations will be required to report to rehab after the use of two (2) SCBA cylinders.    No mandatory limit on exterior operations, the decision as to when to send to rehab, shall be at the discretion of the Incident Commander or Scene Safety Officer based on the assessment of the conditions at the time. Structural Firefighting - Danger rule on heat stress index    Personnel involved in interior firefighting operations will be required to report to rehab after the use of one (1) SCBA cylinder.    Personnel involved in active exterior operations will be required to report to rehab after 45 minutes, or earlier, based on conditions. Haz-Mat Incidents    Personnel wearing total encapsulating suits will rehab and be evaluated during each cylinder change. Technical Rescue, Confined Space Rescue, etc.   The Incident Commander or Safety Officer will make a determination as to when to send personnel to rehab based on type and extent of incident operations in conjunction with the heat stress index. Winter Weather Personnel involved in interior firefighting operations will be required to report to rehab after the use of two (2) SCBA cylinders. There are no mandatory limits on exterior operations. The decision as to when to send personnel to rehab should be based on the existing weather conditions and the observations of the Incident Commander and/or Safety Officer. Extra caution should be taken to prevent frostbite and hypothermia. This will require close monitoring of operating personnel by the Company Officer, Division/Group Supervisor and Rehab Group personnel.
REHAB GROUP OPERATING GUIDELINES Entry and Documentation Personnel will enter Rehab as a company. Personnel assigned to the Rehab Group will record the entry time and the names of all company members on the forms provided. A complete set of vital signs, to include B/P, pulse, respirations, temp, skin conditions, oxygen saturation and carbon monoxide will be taken as soon as possible after arrival in Rehab. Before an individual is released from Rehab, a second set of vital signs shall be taken. The vital signs must be within the recommended guideline established by protocol. Pulse CO-Oximetry:    Normal values: 0-5% (non-smokers) 5-10% (smokers)    SpCO 10-15%: Assess for signs and symptoms of CO poisoning.    SpCO > 15%: Treat with 100% oxygen. Member must have a normal SpCO to be released from rehab. Rehab Group Report Forms The Company Check-In/Out Sheet (OFD-292) (example on page 6) shall be used to record the company number, number of personnel in the company, their time in and time out of Rehab. Rehab Group personnel shall be alert for the following: 1. Personnel complaining of chest pain, dizziness, shortness of breath, weakness, nausea, or headache. 2. General complaints such as cramps, aches and pains. 3. Symptoms of heat or cold related stress 4. Changes in gait, speech or behavior 5. Alertness and orientation to person, place, and time 6. Vital sign considered to be abnormal as established by protocol.
The Incident Rehabilitation Report (OFD-293) shall be used to record the names of individual company members and the company they are working on. There are three rows per individual for recording the time, number of bottles used, vital signs, Rehab medic performing the assessment, and other medical related information. A supply of Rehab forms shall be maintained on each Medic unit, in the Paramedic Shift Supervisor’s car and in the Safety Officer’s car. At the completion of the incident, the Rehab forms will be forwarded to the Safety Officer for post-incident critiques and filing with other incident related reports. Any medical condition or treatment over and above “routine” rehab shall be documented as per ADM 3-2 Injury/Illness Reporting Procedures.
Pre-Hydration Personnel should ensure that they are properly hydrating throughout the shift, prior to an incident.    Body is 60% water    Water is essential for body function and training    Dehydration puts firefighters at increased risk for cardiovascular events, especially during fires    83–91% of firefighters are dehydrated A good sign of proper or lack of hydration is the color of your urine Re-hydration Mandatory During the first air-cylinder change, each individual should consume at least 8 oz. of water. This will require the availability of cool water (no less than 40º F) on Light Air 30. In Rehab: On “Caution” heat stress index days, at least 1 quart of water or other recommended liquids shall be consumed. On “Danger” heat stress index days, at least 2 quarts of water or other recommended liquids shall be consumed. During cold weather, water is still necessary to prevent dehydration. If “warm-up” beverages are consumed, the beverages of choice are chicken or beef broth or hot chocolate. Coffee is not recommended as it interferes with the body’s water conservation capabilities. Recommended During “Danger” heat stress index days, all personnel should drink at least five 8 oz. glasses of water per day, while in company quarters. Rehab Beverages The beverage of choice, other than water, is a 50% solution of a commercial sports drink and water, served at no less than 40º F.
Release from Rehab Rehab personnel shall re-evaluate members prior to their release from rehab to ensure there are no obvious indications that would prevent them from safely performing full-duty activity. Personnel shall be determined to be in one of the following three conditions: 1. Cleared for duty: a. No Companies will leave Rehab until released by the Rehab Group Supervisor. Companies will be released from Rehab as a unit after 20 minutes AND when all company member’s vital signs are within the guidelines recommended above. b. Companies will report to Staging from Rehab, unless conditions at the incident demand immediate re-deployment. Companies may also be released from Rehab to return to quarters with or without their assigned apparatus. 2. Maintained in rehab for further monitoring: a. Exception to company level release: If an individual company member has a medical condition preventing return to active scene duties (i.e. their vital signs do not return to normal or another medical condition develops), the Company will be released and sent to staging with a notification of reduced staffing. The accountability system in use must also reflect this change in personnel. 3. Sent for more definitive medical evaluation/treatment POST-INCIDENT REHABILITATION Although incident scene rehabilitation is important, post-incident rehabilitation is equally important, especially during weather extremes. Replenishing fluids and carbohydrates within the first 30 – 45 minutes after an incident is the most effective approach. If not done during this time period, the body will begin breaking down muscle tissue to convert it to blood sugar to satisfy energy and glycogen replacement requirements. Upon returning to quarters after an incident, Firefighters should begin the rehabilitation process. Fluid Intake    Water    Diluted sports drinks    Hot Chocolate, beef or chicken broth    Avoid coffee, soda pop, fruit juice, etc. as those drinks have negative effects on rehab Food Intake    Fresh or canned fruit (fresh is better)    Complex carbohydrates such as pasta, rice, potatoes and bread Rest    Get some rest; give your body time to “put itself back together”.

Fire Suppression Crews Entering a Marked “Dangerous Structure” If units are dispatched to a structure that is marked “Dangerous Structure” (2x2 neon orange Blocked X with house address above), before entering the structure, the Incident Commander shall make the determination of the best approach for attacking the fire (offensive or defensive). Suppression units should also look for other signs that a structure is unsafe or vacant, even if the structure is not marked as a “Dangerous Structure”. Remember, this could be the first fire at such location and not marked by OFD yet. It is possible the structure could be marked with a condemned or danger notice, be boarded up, have unsecured doors or broken windows, or have a sign in the front yard from the “Land Bank”. The Incident Commander must consider Proper Scene Size-up, Rescue Profile and Risk vs Benefit Analysis. This does not preclude all firefighters on scene from using good judgement before entering the structure. All personnel shall be notified that a structure has been marked “Dangerous”. All crews in the territory of the structure need to visually evaluate the structure, during day light hours, under non-emergency conditions, to have an idea what may be encountered during an emergency incident. It is the goal of emailing all “Vacant/Dangerous” forms to all suppression personnel in the effort for first alarm companies to be proactive and already size-up such structure prior to additional fires (i.e. access, hydrants, exposures, offensive/defensive strategies)
SAFETY AND WELLNESS 8-0 POST-INCIDENT ANALYSIS PROCEDURES REVISION HISTORY REVISION #    REVISION DESCRIPTION       DATE REVISED       AUTHOR        REVIEWED BY       APPROVED BY N/A               New SOP                April 2019     Langlois 888     McCaw 897          Olsen 640 1           OFD 401 Form Update         May 2024      Thorson 1189      Bradley 923      Bossman 729 PURPOSE The purpose of this Post-Incident Analysis (PIA) procedure is to provide mechanisms for objectively analyzing firefighter serious injuries/line of duty deaths (LODDs), close calls, multi- company, complex, unusual, multi-jurisdictional, or large scale fire rescue operations in a post emergency environment. To additionally provide a review and development process through which improved methods and operations may be realized, as well as, to identify ways to improve effectiveness, training, and standard operating procedures. The PIA process is an opportunity for the department to become better as a whole, rather than an opportunity to place blame or be punitive in nature. POLICY PIA Types 1. Hot Wash: A hot wash is typically performed as quickly as possible after the incident or event. It is the most informal post incident review process and involves a quick discussion with participants to identify strengths and weaknesses of the response. It should be performed by the Incident Commander at each working fire or significant incident. a. OFD 401: An OFD 401 Post Fire Incident Analysis Form shall be completed by the Incident Commander within two (2) calendar days of a working fire or significant incident. 2. Debrief: An incident debrief should be performed the shift after the incident, and should include all personnel who responded. It is conducted by the Incident Commander or Operations Assistant Chief. It may include Division personnel (Training, Safety, EMS, etc.) as needed. A debrief is performed after any working fire or significant incident as deemed necessary by the Incident Commander, Incident Safety Officer or the Operations Assistant Chief. It can be a formal or informal discussion for the purpose of identifying strengths and weaknesses of the response. Everyone in attendance should be allowed to contribute to the discussion. 3. Formal PIA: A formal PIA is an in depth, written analysis of all aspects of an incident. It should be conducted by trained personnel who are part of a pre-designated PIA Team. It may be initiated anytime deemed necessary by the Assistant Fire Chief or Fire Chief.

Triggering Events for a PIA A hot wash should be conducted after every working fire or significant incident. A debrief may be important after incidents such as, but not limited to:    Structure fires (single alarm)    Level 1 Multi-Casualty Incident (MCI)    Level 1 or 2 Hazardous Materials Incident    Complicated extrications    Technical Rescue A formal PIA may be triggered by the following types of incidents:    Major incident (noteworthy, unusual, large dollar loss, etc.)    Close call, MAYDAY, serious injury or death of a member of the OFD, including as a result of a vehicle accident.    Help-A-Firefighter incidents    Multiple civilian casualties    Second alarm or greater incidents    Extraordinary fire loss (e.g. total loss of a commercial structure)    Incidents that require long-term commitment of resources    Level 2 or 3 MCI    Level 3 Hazardous Materials incident    Complicated technical rescue incident    Incidents requiring Douglas County Emergency Operations Center activation FORMAL WRITTEN POST-INCIDENT ANALYSIS PROCEDURES A formal written PIA should be developed through the combined efforts of trained personnel assembled in a PIA Team, led by a Chief Officer, typically either the Training Division or Safety/Wellness Division Battalion Chief. Personnel who were involved in the incident should not be a part of the PIA team if possible. Additional subject matter experts can be included to assist the PIA Team as appropriate, such as Fleet Maintenance personnel for an apparatus accident, or special operations personnel if the incident involves special operations aspects. If the incident involves a possible criminal investigation the PIA Team shall coordinate with FIU, OPD or any other involved law enforcement or criminal investigation agency. Formal PIA Initial Actions 1. For any incident that might have a Post Incident Analysis (PIA) conducted on it, especially any incident that involves a close call, firefighter emergency, MAYDAY, injury or line of duty death, trained PIA personnel should be assigned, or called back if necessary, to respond to the scene of the incident as soon as possible while the incident is still ongoing
